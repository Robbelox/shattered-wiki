History

Founded a few hundred years ago as a minor Achaean colony, the city that became the worldâ€™s desire had small beginnings. It was founded by a group of exiles from a minor Achaean city, who wanted to gain power in a new land. They started by raiding the natives and their neighbouring colonies, gaining goods and slaves that were essential to start the new colony.

Over time, the colony grew into a city, and was located on a principal trade zone jutting out into the southern seas. It came onto the map soon after, as traders would routinely stop there to trade goods, and take on food and water. But their principal good of value was slaves. The city leaders would continue to conquer the natives, taking on the much needed slaves which they could then sell on to the traders, and soon the city grew wealthy.

Along with this new found wealth, came a rise in ambition. The city was wracked with internal struggles, as the inhabitants used their money to try and gain power, causing a power vacuum as the great houses fought between one another, killing off their opposition. Finally, a great general known as Tiberian seized power, expelling the landed houses of the city and taking their riches. He was quick to make use of this opportunity, using this money to buy mercenaries, and taking over the surrounding Achaean colonies, letting them choose between subjugation, or the city being burnt to the ground.

Before long, the entire peninsula was his, and he crowned himself as the leader of a new nation. The Empire of Tiberia.

Tiberia was set apart from the other Achaean colonies by its rigid military tradition. The best way to rise through the ranks was to serve in the army, and win glory against the surrounding natives and colonies. The only way to survive in the city was to win battles, and Tiberia spent the next century taking over as much as they could, subjugating all of the Achaean people, and the natives who lived upon the southern sea coast.

Soon, the city of Tiberia became the new centre of the continent, with traders from across the world flocking there to trade lucrative goods from across the Empire. The Imperial Government was centred there, and the military would parade there whenever they won a triumph against a foreign foe.

The Tiberian Legion was once considered the most fearsome military in the world. Consisting of a meticulously organised and disciplined force divided by the decimal, using fearsome tactics and superior equipment, they managed to conquer much of the continent in a matter of centuries. Citizens of the Tiberian Empire are drafted to serve a minimum of 10 years of service, followed by a pension and land grant. This system encouraged military service for the poor, and meant that they had no lack of recruits. Of course, this system drove on the war machine by needing an ever growing amount of land to hand out, meaning that the Tiberian Empire had to continue growing to support the system.

This military tradition lives on past the Shattering, and each Varian citizen is educated in the art of war.

However, the Tiberians were the ones worst affected by the Shattering. Many of the Tiberian cities and colonies across the world were wiped out instantly by divine disasters brought on by the godâ€™s wrath, causing cities to shake and break apart, great winds strong enough to overturn buildings, and great waves as tall as mountains hitting the coasts. Not even the royal family were spared, and the direct descendents of Tiberian himself perished in the events.

Only a few Tiberians survived, those from the city of Vara having left the cities and entered the mountains before the worst of the Shattering occured. It was far from the lush farmlands and trade centres that they had lived in before, but they managed to continue on despite the desolate area.

Now the last Tiberian citizens left in the continent, the people strive to reclaim what was once theirs, and to re-establish the Tiberian Empire in a new age, now reborn like a Phoenix as the Varian Empire.

Religion

The Tiberians worship a religion that is very similar to that of the Achaeans, being based off of the same traditions.

At the center of Pelonian is the pantheon of deities who were said to live on Mount Pelion, the highest mountain in Achaea. From their perch, they ruled every aspect of human life. Pelionian gods and goddesses looked like men and women (though they could change themselves into animals and other things) and were â€“ as many myths recounted â€“ vulnerable to human foibles and passions.

The traditional Achaean gods are highly diverse, each of them representing one part of nature, or humans. This can vary from being an emotion, like a god of anger, to a nature spirit whose lifeforce is tied to a certain river.

However, the Tiberian gods are very different in style. The gods are much more militarised, to match the centralised nation. The God of War is the highest god in the Tiberian pantheon, rather than a god of the sea as in the Achaean one. The God of Wisdom, is primarily a god of Military Tactics. The good of the forge is known as the creator of weapons, rather than feats of engineering. In these examples, you can see that the gods are suited to one thing, and one thing only. War.

Customs

Icy Greetings

The Tiberians have a custom whereby they drop cold water onto the head of any foreign visitor to their lands. It is said that this strange tradition started when a rival nation sent an unwashed tribesman to negotiate a peace treaty unsuccessfully, acting as a slight against the Emperor.

As such, the Tiberians have never dropped the act, and have continued to douse foreigners ever since, even installing fountains at the entrances to cities so that it always followed.

Godess of War

Within the capital of the Tiberian Empire is the magnificent Temple of Beluna, the goddess of war. This site is the most important shrine within the Empire, and as such is known as one of the 5 Wonders of the Pre-Shattering world. It is said to have reached to the heavens, and to have shone with light each day, being built from the finest marble on the continent. The priests who give their lives to the goddess wound their own arms or legs as a blood sacrifice, to show their devotion to the deity.

Each family worships a specific god and will pray or honor their god as a family each week.
