Bastaernian Empire

History

Ancient Tribes (... - 0)

The Bastaerni were a highly tribalised society, illustrating both the strengths and weaknesses of extreme decentralisation. They inhabited the vast forest lands of the south, leaving their territory primarily to trade with foreign peoples for non-local goods. Throughout most of their early history, they remained highly insular, conducting raids only against those who strayed too close to the forest borders.

Bastaernian society was divided into hundreds of petty kings, each ruling a tribe or even a subdivision of one. This highly decentralised structure led to frequent conflicts between tribes, and when combined with a strongly warlike culture, resulted in near-constant internal warfare. As a consequence, the Bastaerni were often too occupied with fighting one another to mount sustained campaigns against external enemies.

On several occasions, however, the tribes united under a single leader, usually in response to large-scale foreign invasions. These rare unifiers were remembered as harsh and uncompromising rulers, willing to employ brutality to impose unity. Such periods of consolidation never lasted longer than a single generation. Upon the death of the unifying king, tribal autonomy reasserted itself and internal conflict resumed.

The only tribes known to have achieved lasting unification were the Osterwalde and the Suaring, despite being ancient enemies and blood rivals. Their mutual hostility was rooted in myth, tracing back to the legendary founders of each tribe, whose conflict was believed to have set the pattern for generations of rivalry.

As northern foreigners began establishing colonies along the northern coast, the Bastaerni developed an ambivalent relationship with them. At times, they raided colonial forts and settlements; at others, they engaged in trade. Through this exchange, the Bastaerni entered wider continental networks, exporting furs in return for luxury goods such as silks, dyes, and metals.

The Achaeans and Tiberians frequently attempted to manipulate Bastaernian politics by financing and arming rival tribes, deliberately exacerbating internal divisions. Despite these efforts, neither power succeeded in conquering the southern forests. Military expeditions routinely ended in defeat at the hands of skilled Bastaernian raiders, or collapsed due to supply shortages in devastated, food-poor terrain. As a result, the Bastaerni maintained their independence throughout the pre-imperial period.

Great Shattering (0 - 10)

The Great Shattering reached the Bastaerni later than most continental societies, largely due to their geographic isolation and limited integration into imperial systems. When it did arrive, its effects were devastating. The Bastaerni possessed little resistance to the plagues that had long circulated through the Tiberian Empire and surrounding regions. Entire tribes were wiped out as disease spread rapidly through coastal settlements and inland routes. Mass flight followed, as survivors moved southward into the Bastaerni heartlands. This displacement often worsened the crisis, unintentionally spreading infection further into previously unaffected regions.

In response, several tribal kings implemented harsh but effective containment measures. These typically involved the execution of the infected and the strict isolation of corpses from the wider population. Through such measures, a small number of rulers survived the Shattering and absorbed neighbouring tribes that had lost their leadership entirely.

These new kings remained in the southern regions for many years, until the plagues subsided and surviving populations developed partial immunity. The cost of survival, however, was immense. The northern forests had been largely destroyed by the divine disasters associated with the Shattering. Climatic changes eventually rendered the southern lands uninhabitable, forcing the surviving Bastaerni back north into territories now largely devoid of human life.

From this upheaval emerged two dominant successor kingdoms: the Osterwalde and the Suaring, ruled by former rival dynasties. Both settled along the northern coasts and increasingly turned their attention outward, carrying their ancestral rivalry into a new geopolitical context.

First Lude (10 ~ 100)

Pre-Unification Kingdoms

Osterwalde

The Osterwalde traced their origins to the legendary king Walde, who claimed descent from the earth god Aemok. According to tradition, Walde wrestled and slew a mountain lion sent by a jealous goddess whom he had rejected, saving his kin and taking the beast’s pelt as a trophy. The lion subsequently became the symbol of the Osterwalde banner, representing victory over divine opposition and the inherent strength of the clan. Osterwalde emerged as one of the most powerful Bastaernian kingdoms. Its rulers emphasised martial discipline and territorial defence, preserving ancient tribal customs while aggressively contesting borders with rival clans.

Suaring

The Suaring descended from the legendary king Suara, who claimed lineage from the god of rivers. Suara was remembered as both the founder of his people and as a challenger of the divine. According to myth, he boasted of being the greatest swimmer, breath-holder, and fisherman in the world, provoking the local river god, who took the form of a turtle.

The god challenged Suara to a series of contests, wagering the finest woman in the world against Suara’s life. The trials lasted for weeks, but Suara ultimately prevailed, defeating the river god and securing both his survival and his legendary status. From this victory, the Suaring clan claimed its origin.

In the post-Shattering period, the Suaring kingdom became known for adaptability and cunning. Its rulers exploited river networks for trade and raiding, while sustaining a long-standing blood feud with neighbouring powers, most notably Osterwalde.

Unification

Kaiser: Auduin

Following the Varian–Prythonian War, King Auduin of Osterwalde turned his attention toward the Suaring kingdom. His approach mirrored earlier attempts at consolidation within Osterwalde itself during the period known as the Swift Panic. Guided by his advisor, the landowner Æthelstan, Auduin promoted the idea of a shared Bastaernian identity, founding a pan-Bastaernian university and proposing jointly administered cities.

Negotiations culminated in a historic meeting between King Auduin and Queen Mara of the Suaring. Rather than conquest, the two rulers agreed to dissolve their respective crowns and establish an entirely new political entity.

During the unification ceremony, the earth god Aemok was said to have appeared, enraged by the peaceful joining of rival peoples and demanding blood. Æthelstan stepped forward to face the god, expressing both regret and satisfaction at the fulfilment of his vision. When Aemok reached for his axe, it was found missing. Auduin struck Æthelstan with the divine weapon, severing his right hand rather than killing him, and declared that this sacrifice would be the only blood spilled for Bastaernia.

Auduin was proclaimed Kaiser of the Bastaernian Empire, and Æthelstan became his closest advisor, remembered thereafter as the Kaiser’s “right hand.”

Under Auduin’s rule, elements of foreign administrative practice were combined with traditional tribal values. The empire was divided into semi-autonomous states, each governed by its own leader. This structure secured long-term stability and marked the transition of the Bastaerni from fragmented tribes to an imperial polity.

Religion

Aemocism

Most of the surviving mythology centres on the plights of the gods and their interaction with various other beings, such as humanity and the jötnar, beings who may be friends, lovers, foes or family members of the gods. The cosmos in Bastaerni mythology consists of Seven Worlds that flank a central tree, Læraðr. Units of time and elements of the cosmology are personified as deities or beings.

The source texts mention numerous gods, such as:
- The battle-axe-wielding, humanity-protecting earth god Aemok, who relentlessly fights his foes;
- His son, Loki, who craftily pursues knowledge throughout the worlds and bestowed among humanity the runic alphabet;
- The beautiful, feathered cloak-clad goddess who rides to battle to choose among the slain;
- The vengeful, hunting goddess, who prefers the wolf howls of the winter mountains to the seashore;
- The god whose weather and farming associations bring peace and pleasure to humanity;
- The widowed goddess, who keeps apples that grant eternal youthfulness.

Elyshi

The Elyshi are a Sect of Aemocism beginning in the South east of Basternia. They believe in the traditional pantheon of Aemocism with a stronger value of Loki and greater worship.

Customs

Warrior Ethos

It is rare for the Bastaernians people to be educated, most are born into poor, working families, so they learn trade over education. This differs for those with more riches, but those still have basic education in comparison with some other nations. Instead of brains, most make up for it in brawn and learn more of fighting and working hard than any other kingdom.

Nomenclature

Bastaernian names reflect the tribal, nature-bound heritage of the people, often rooted in Old Germanic and Norse influences that evoke strength, kinship with the land, valor in battle, or ties to the divine and mythical. Names are not mere labels but carry aspirations for endurance, wisdom from the forests, or prowess against foes, mirroring a society where survival and honor are paramount. References to animals, elements like rivers or mountains, and ancestral deeds underscore their insular, warlike culture. Numerical or positional elements are rare, but familial bonds and heroic traits dominate.

Individuals often bear a single given name, sometimes augmented by a clan descriptor or epithet, emphasizing tribal loyalty. Male names lean toward themes of protection, leadership, and ferocity, aligning with raiding and defense; female names more frequently suggest grace, resilience, or natural harmony, though both genders share connotations of unyielding spirit.
