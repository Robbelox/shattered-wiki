The Nature Goddess of Pakaroo

While many Pakari people follow the words and teachings of the Blood God, Pakaroo is home to many tribes and peoples. Other gods have faded into the background and might not be as well-known as the devout and vocal followers of the Blood God. However, nature remains a universal force, found everywhere‚Äîfrom the smallest flower to the largest creatures and trees in the world.

Many tribes and clans across the world have deities dedicated to nature, and most of these are goddesses. The Pakari people worship six distinct nature goddesses, each belonging to a major tribe or culture. These goddesses play vital roles in protecting the Pakari people, strengthening their harvests, blessing them with peaceful seasons, and ensuring the flourishing of their herds.

Goddess of art, games, dance, summer, pleasure, flowers, and song ‚Äî X≈çchipilli belongs to a tribe within the Pakari people called the "Aztecs." She is the sister of Xochiquetzal.

Her name translates to "Flower Princess" in the language of her people. She is also revered as the patron saint of courtesans and those who identify as queer in any form.

X≈çchipilli welcomes all who seek her as their Saint, embracing them graciously and openly.

For those wishing to make an offering, flowers are the best choice. She also blesses anyone who treats nature with kindness and respect.

Xochiquetzal

She is the goddess of fertility, beauty, female pleasure and power, protection of young mothers, pregnancy, childbirth, vegetation, crops, animals, and women‚Äôs crafts.

Her name means "Precious Feather" in the language of her people. Xochiquetzal is revered as the protector of all women. She is always depicted as an alluring and youthful woman, richly adorned and symbolically associated with vegetation, particularly flowers.

Xochiquetzal welcomes all who wish to have her as their Saint, embracing them with open arms and a kind heart. She is the sister of X≈çchipilli.

Offerings: Flowers, saplings, liquor, honey, and feathers are graciously accepted.

Every eight years, worshipers honor her with a festival where they wear animal and flower masks in celebration.

Pachamama

She is the goddess of planting, harvesting, earthquakes, and mountains.

Her name, in the language of the "Inca" people, translates literally to "Mother Earth" or "Earth Mother." Pachamama is benevolent and generous with her gifts, but problems arise when people take too much from nature, as this is akin to taking too much from Pachamama herself. To honor her, people must give back to the nature she provides.

Offerings: Potatoes, cocoa beans, cookies, wheat, and bread are all fitting tributes. A sacrifice of llamas is considered a particularly wonderful and generous gift.

If you wish for Pachamama to be your Saint, she may hesitate at first. However, through consistent prayer and offerings over at least one week, she will accept you as her follower, blessing you with her love and guidance.

Asintmah

She is the goddess of the earth, all nature, and animals.

Her name means "Mother of All Living" in the languages of the native northern tribes.

It is said that Asintmah birthed the first of every animal that walks the Earth. As the first woman to walk the Earth, she is known for her kindness, gentleness, and soft heart toward all who pray to her.

Offerings: Do not offer sacrifices. Instead, gifts such as wool, honey, feathers, scutes, and similar items are what she desires most.

If you wish for Asintmah to be your Saint, refrain from killing any animals for one week and pray to her. She will then come to you, offering her love and power.

Asintmah is followed by many tribes and is known by various names across the northern tribes. However, all agree that she was the first woman to walk the Earth and is the kindest of all their goddesses.

Papatuanuku

She is the goddess of all creatures living on Earth, no matter their size.

In the Maori or Polynesian tribes of Pakaroo, her name translates to "Earth Mother." Papatuanuku is revered as the protector of animals and women.

Unlike other goddesses, she is not always kind. Her punishments manifest as hurricanes, famines, and droughts, though her most loyal followers remain untouched by such wrath.

Offerings: Water, feathers, meat, bread, or liquor are the most fitting gifts.

If you wish for Papatuanuku to be your Saint, it may take time and dedication. You must first devote yourself to her through daily prayer and consistent offerings before receiving her blessing.

Papatuanuku is also recognized as the mother of all other gods in Maori traditions. Many of her children embody aspects of nature, but she reigns supreme over them all.

Curupira

She is the goddess of animals and the weak, though many perceive her as a demon rather than a goddess.

Her name means "Covered in Blisters" in the language of the Brazilian tribes that worship her.

Curupira is not seen as "beautiful" like the other goddesses of Pakaroo. She is cursed with fur and blisters, leading some to describe her as monstrous.

Curupira is known for killing poachers and those who prey on the innocent. Her altars are often found deep in the woods, and she is said to wander the forests of Pakaroo at night, ensuring that the "undeserving" and "unworthy" cannot enter.

Offerings: Saplings, honey, bread, and sugar are welcomed. Do NOT offer animals, blood, or hides ‚Äî she will respond with anger.

If you wish for Curupira to be your Saint, be cautious. She is quick to accept devotion but just as quick to anger if disrespected.

Worship and Harmony in Pakaroo

The six goddesses are worshiped at the Northern Temple in Pakaroo, also known as the "Great Mother's Temple." They are revered for their kindness, provided they are respected and their rules are followed.

These goddesses share their powers equally, embodying the harmony Pakaroo strives to achieve among its diverse tribes and peoples.
