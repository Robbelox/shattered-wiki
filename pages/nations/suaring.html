Suaring

History

Ancient Tribes (... - 0)

The Suaring traced their origins to the legendary king Suara, who claimed descent from the god of rivers. Suara was remembered as both the founder of his people and as a challenger of the divine. According to myth, Suara boasted of being the greatest swimmer, the one who could hold his breath the longest, and the best fisherman in the world. The local river god, who took the form of a turtle, became offended by this immense pride and challenged Suara to a contest.

The god wagered that if Suara won, he would be given the finest woman in the world, but if Suara lost, he would drown in the river. The two fought hard across a series of trials that lasted for weeks, testing swimming ability, breath-holding endurance, and fishing skill. In the end, Suara emerged victorious, defeating the river god and securing both his survival and his legendary bride. From this triumph, the Suaring clan claimed its origin and derived its identity as a people favoured by cunning and perseverance over divine power.

The Suaring were part of the broader Bastaerni peoples, a highly tribalised society that inhabited the vast forest lands of the south. The Bastaerni were divided into hundreds of petty kings, each ruling a tribe or even a subdivision of one. This highly decentralised structure led to frequent conflicts between tribes, and when combined with a strongly warlike culture, resulted in near-constant internal warfare. The Suaring participated fully in this cycle of raiding and feuding, leaving their territory primarily to trade with foreign peoples or to conduct raids against those who strayed too close to the forest borders.

Throughout their early history, the Suaring maintained a blood rivalry with the Osterwalde people, another major Bastaerni tribe. This enmity stretched back to time immemorial, rooted in the mythical conflict between the two tribes' legendary founders—Suara and Walde. The rivalry shaped Suaring identity and military focus for generations, ensuring that much of their martial tradition was honed in conflicts with their closest cultural relatives rather than distant foreign powers.

On several occasions throughout history, strong leaders managed to unite the scattered Bastaerni tribes, usually in response to large-scale foreign invasions. These rare unifiers were remembered as harsh and uncompromising rulers, willing to employ brutality to impose unity. The Suaring participated in some of these temporary confederations, but such periods of consolidation never lasted longer than a single generation. Upon the death of the unifying king, tribal autonomy reasserted itself and internal conflict resumed.

As northern foreigners, particularly the Achaeans and Tiberians, began establishing colonies along the northern coast, the Suaring developed an ambivalent relationship with them. At times, they raided colonial forts and settlements; at others, they engaged in trade. Through this exchange, the Suaring entered wider continental networks, exporting furs in return for luxury goods such as silks, dyes, and metals. The Achaeans and Tiberians frequently attempted to manipulate Bastaernian politics by financing and arming rival tribes, deliberately exacerbating internal divisions. Despite these efforts, neither power succeeded in conquering the southern forests, and the Suaring maintained their independence throughout the pre-imperial period.

Great Shattering (0 - 10)

The Great Shattering reached the Suaring and other Bastaerni peoples later than most continental societies, largely due to their geographic isolation and limited integration into imperial systems. When it did arrive, its effects were devastating. The Suaring possessed little resistance to the plagues that had long circulated through the Tiberian Empire and surrounding regions. Entire villages and clans were wiped out as disease spread rapidly through coastal settlements and inland routes.

Mass flight followed, as Suaring survivors moved southward into the Bastaerni heartlands. This displacement often worsened the crisis, unintentionally spreading infection further into previously unaffected regions. In response, several Suaring chieftains implemented harsh but effective containment measures. These typically involved the execution of the infected and the strict isolation of corpses from the wider population. Through such measures, a small number of leaders survived the Shattering and absorbed neighbouring tribes and clans that had lost their leadership entirely.

These surviving Suaring remained in the southern regions for many years, until the plagues subsided and populations developed partial immunity. The cost of survival, however, was immense. The northern forests that had been the Suaring homeland had been largely destroyed by the divine disasters associated with the Shattering. Climatic changes eventually rendered the southern lands uninhabitable, forcing the surviving Suaring back north into territories now largely devoid of human life.

From this upheaval emerged the Suaring kingdom, one of two dominant Bastaerni successor states alongside their ancient rivals, the Osterwalde. Both settled along the northern coasts and increasingly turned their attention outward, carrying their ancestral rivalry into a new geopolitical context.

First Lude (10 ~ 100)

The Suaring emerged as one of the most powerful Bastaernian kingdoms in the post-Shattering world. In the post-Shattering period, the Suaring kingdom became known for adaptability and cunning. Its rulers exploited river networks for trade and raiding, drawing on the legendary heritage of Suara's mastery of waterways and aquatic skills. The kingdom maintained a long-standing blood feud with the Osterwalde, their closest neighbours and oldest enemies.

The kingdom maintained the traditional Bastaerni warrior ethos. Education remained rare, with most Suaring born into working families where they learned trades rather than formal learning. Those with greater wealth received basic education, but it remained limited in comparison with other nations. Instead, the Suaring made up for lack of formal education through physical prowess and martial training, learning fighting and hard work above all else.

The legendary heritage of king Suara and the divine favour claimed through descent from the river god remained central to Suaring political legitimacy and cultural identity. The story of Suara's triumph over the turtle-formed deity through weeks of grueling competition served as a cultural touchstone, emphasising the values of persistence, skill, and the ability to overcome seemingly insurmountable challenges through determination rather than brute force alone.

Under Mara (? - 10)

Queen: Mara

Unification with Osterwalde

Following the Varian–Prythonian War, King Auduin of Osterwalde pursued an unprecedented diplomatic initiative toward the Suaring kingdom. Rather than continuing the ancient blood feud through military conquest, Auduin and his advisor Æthelstan proposed a peaceful union. Negotiations culminated in a historic meeting between King Auduin and Queen Mara of the Suaring.

The two rulers agreed to dissolve their respective crowns and establish an entirely new political entity: the Bastaernian Empire. This decision represented the first time in recorded history that the Suaring and Osterwalde had peacefully joined rather than fought. For the Suaring, this meant abandoning centuries of enmity rooted in the mythical conflict between Suara and Walde, a profound cultural transformation that required Queen Mara to persuade her people that unity offered greater advantages than continued rivalry.

During the unification ceremony, the earth god Aemok was said to have appeared, enraged by the peaceful joining of rival peoples and demanding blood sacrifice to sanctify the new order. Æthelstan stepped forward to face the god, expressing both regret and satisfaction at the fulfilment of his vision for Bastaernian unity. When Aemok reached for his divine axe, it was found missing—Auduin had taken it. The king struck Æthelstan with the weapon, severing his advisor's right hand rather than killing him. Auduin declared that this sacrifice would be the only blood spilled for Bastaernia.

Auduin was proclaimed Kaiser of the Bastaernian Empire, and the Suaring transformed from an independent kingdom into a constituent part of a larger imperial structure. Queen Mara's role in the unified empire and the political arrangements that preserved Suaring autonomy ensured that the union was more than simple conquest, though the Osterwalde remained the dominant force in the new polity.

The Bastaernian Empire

Under Auduin's rule as Kaiser, elements of foreign administrative practice were combined with traditional Suaring and Bastaerni tribal values. The empire was divided into semi-autonomous states, each governed by its own leader. This structure allowed the preservation of local Suaring traditions and leadership patterns while creating overarching imperial institutions. The arrangement secured long-term stability and marked the transition of the Suaring from a tribal kingdom to a constituent part of an imperial polity.

The Suaring maintained their distinctive cultural identity within the empire, including their reverence for the legendary deeds of king Suara and the river god heritage. Their expertise in riverine trade and waterway navigation continued to contribute to imperial economic development, while their historical rivalry with the Osterwalde gradually transformed into a more cooperative relationship under the unifying framework established by Auduin and Æthelstan.

Religion

Aemocism

The Suaring practiced Aemocism, the traditional Bastaerni religion. Most of the surviving mythology centred on the plights of the gods and their interaction with various other beings, such as humanity and the jötnar, beings who might be friends, lovers, foes, or family members of the gods. The cosmos in Bastaerni mythology consisted of Seven Worlds that flanked a central tree, Læraðr. Units of time and elements of the cosmology were personified as deities or beings.

While the Suaring shared the broader Bastaerni pantheon with the Osterwalde and other tribes, their particular devotion to the god of rivers reflected their legendary founder's heritage. The story of Suara's contest with the river god in turtle form remained central to Suaring religious practice, emphasising themes of human cunning and perseverance against divine power. Other significant deities included Aemok, the battle-axe-wielding, humanity-protecting earth god who relentlessly fought his foes; his son Loki, who craftily pursued knowledge throughout the worlds and bestowed among humanity the runic alphabet; the beautiful, feathered cloak-clad goddess who rode to battle to choose among the slain; the vengeful hunting goddess who preferred the wolf howls of the winter mountains to the seashore; the god whose weather and farming associations brought peace and pleasure to humanity; and the widowed goddess who kept apples that granted eternal youthfulness.

Customs

Warrior Ethos

Education remained rare among the Suaring people. Most were born into poor, working families and learned trades rather than formal education. This differed for those with greater wealth, but even they received only basic education in comparison with other nations. Instead of formal learning, most Suaring compensated through physical strength and combat training, learning fighting and hard work above all else.

This emphasis on martial prowess over scholarship reflected the Suaring's forest-dwelling heritage and the constant tribal warfare that had characterised their pre-Shattering existence. Even after unification into the Bastaernian Empire, the Suaring maintained this traditional value system, producing skilled warriors and craftsmen rather than scholars or administrators. Their legendary founder's reputation for physical excellence—swimming ability, breath-holding endurance, and fishing skill—continued to shape cultural ideals of what constituted worthy achievement.

Nomenclature

Suaring names reflected the tribal, nature-bound heritage of the people, often rooted in Old Germanic and Norse influences that evoked strength, kinship with the land, valor in battle, or ties to the divine and mythical. Names were not mere labels but carried aspirations for endurance, wisdom from the forests, or prowess against foes, mirroring a society where survival and honor were paramount. References to animals, elements like rivers or mountains, and ancestral deeds underscored their insular, warlike culture.

Individuals often bore a single given name, sometimes augmented by a clan descriptor or epithet, emphasising tribal loyalty. Male names leaned toward themes of protection, leadership, and ferocity, aligning with raiding and defence; female names more frequently suggested grace, resilience, or natural harmony, though both genders shared connotations of unyielding spirit.
