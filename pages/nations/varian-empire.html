# Varian Empire ## About === --- ## History ### Tiberia (... - 0) The origins of what would become the Varian Empire lie in the distant past, in a minor [[achaea|Achaean]] colony founded by exiles seeking power in a new land. Departing from one of the smaller cities of Achaea, they established their settlement through raiding the natives and neighboring colonies, acquiring the goods and slaves essential to their foothold. Over time, this colony grew into a city strategically located on a principal trade zone jutting out into the southern seas. The settlement came onto the map as traders routinely stopped there to trade goods and take on food and water. But their principal commodity of value was slaves. The city leaders continued to conquer the natives, taking the much-needed slaves which they could then sell to traders, and soon the city grew wealthy. #### The Rise of Tiberian Along with newfound wealth came a rise in ambition. The city was wracked with internal struggles as inhabitants used their money to try and gain power, causing a power vacuum as the great houses fought one another, killing off their opposition. Finally, a great general known as [[Tiberian]] seized power, expelling the landed houses of the city and taking their riches. He used this money to buy mercenaries and took over the surrounding Achaean colonies, letting them choose between subjugation or having the city burnt to the ground. Soon after, he crowned himself as the leader of a new nation: the Empire of Tiberia. Tiberia, despite the impressive military it commanded, often had little use for it. Due to its well structured government and central location, people came to them. Towns would offer themselves to the growing nation for its protection and just governing, eager to share in the prosperity Tiberia provided. Before long, the entire peninsula was his. #### The Tiberian Military State Tiberia was set apart from other Achaean colonies by its rigid military tradition. The best way to rise through the ranks was to serve in the army and win glory, and the only way to truly survive in the city was to win battles. They spent the next century taking over as much as they could, subjugating the Achaean colonies and the natives along the southern sea coast. The Tiberian Legion was once considered the most fearsome military in the world: meticulously disciplined, organized along decimal lines, and built on devastating tactics and superior equipment. Citizens were drafted to serve a minimum of ten years, after which they received a pension and a land grant. This encouraged service among the poor and ensured a steady stream of recruits, but it also fed the war machine: an ever-growing supply of land was needed to sustain the system, and so the Empire had to keep expanding simply to support itself. As Tiberia's reach grew, so did its prominence. The city became the new center of the continent, with traders from across the world bringing lucrative goods to its markets. The Imperial Government was seated there, and the legions would parade through its streets whenever they returned in triumph. For hundreds of years the Empire grew, and with it, its power. Unfortunately, the old adage rang true: the larger they are, the harder they fall. In a great disaster that swept the land, almost the entirety of the Tiberian Empire was destroyed in a matter of days. - - - ### Great Shattering (0 - 10) Main Article: [[Great Shattering]] The Tiberians were the ones worst affected by the Great Shattering. Divine disasters brought on by the gods' wrath shook cities apart, raised winds strong enough to overturn buildings, and sent waves as tall as mountains crashing into the coasts. Many of the Tiberian cities and colonies across the world were wiped out instantly. Not even the royal family was spared, as the direct descendants of Tiberian himself perished in the chaos. The social order that had held for centuries collapsed in a matter of days. Those who had once been rich watched their villas stripped and their farmlands scorched, while peasants who had survived turned on their former owners, taking out a lifetime of resentment. The civilized world reverted to its primitive roots, and what order remained was preyed upon by the barbarian tribes of the north, who conducted raids deep into the broken south. Among those who survived was [[aurelius-vara|Aurelius]], second son of Septimus Vara, head of one of the most powerful families in the old Empire. Born into privilege but treated as a spare rather than an heir, he had been banished to a military academy on the mountainous northern border after being caught sneaking out to engage in petty theft with street children. The academy's harsh discipline forged him into an exceptional soldier, though he harbored deep resentment toward his family. When he reached his name day and returned to [[Vara]] to choose his patron deity, Aurelius scaled the high staircase to the [[Temple of Beluna]], goddess of war. He knelt before her altar and sealed his devotion by marking his face with a knife, letting blood flow past his eyes without flinching. The goddess's blessing altered his destiny forever. A few nights later, the world began to shake. Aurelius fled with a few younger boys, hiding in the woods and surviving on what they could find. When they emerged a month later, they found only ruins — every town empty, only gangs of bandits moving through the wreckage. Even the great city of Vara had been reduced to a shadow of its former glory. A pestilence known as the [[Nasyrik Plague]] stalked the survivors, deforming most into a wretched people who would come to be known as [[Mutes]]. Aurelius turned his back on the ruins and led his group into the mountains, gathering survivors along the way. His leadership saved many lives, and he was soon elected first Emperor of what remained of the Varian people. Vara's isolation in the mountains had spared them the worst of the Nasyrik pandemic, and from that refuge, the last Tiberians vowed to rebuild. Like a phoenix, their empire was reborn as the Varian Empire. - - - ### First Lude (10 ~ 100) #### Aurelian Era (10) **Emperor:** [[Aurelius Vara]] Aurelius led the Varian people from the highlands, bringing with him survivors of the greatest catastrophe the world had known. Under his rule, the Varian Empire began its long struggle to reclaim what was once theirs and re-establish their prominence in a shattered world. From this defensible position, Aurelius worked to establish the foundations of Varian power. His devotion to [[Beluna]] remained absolute, and the goddess's blessing seemed to guide his decisions as he navigated the treacherous politics of the post-shattering world. On the 2nd of Early Summer, 10 AE, Aurelius stepped down from the imperial throne and appointed the blacksmith [[julius-sapienti-ex-vara|Julius]] as his successor. The reasons for his abdication remained largely private, though some speculated that the burdens of rebuilding an empire from ruins had taken their toll. Regardless, his transition of power was peaceful and orderly, reflecting the Varian commitment to stability and proper succession. **Notable Varians:** #### Julian Era (10 - ?) **Emperor:** [[Julius sapienti ex Vara]] Julius sapienti inherited an empire still finding its footing in the new world. Where Aurelius had been a warrior blessed by the goddess of war, Julius brought a different approach to leadership. He would be more hands on, having come from humble unknown origins. **Notable Varians:** - - - ### Ludes (100 ~ 900) #### Centuries of Struggle For centuries, the Varians were regarded by much of the world as an upstart — a band of stubborn fools playing at nationhood. They were rarely powerful enough to threaten any established power, and cycled through many ways of living in the attempt: cave dwellers, seafaring nomads, a near-extinct cult clinging to old rites. What confused outsiders most was not their failure, but their persistence. The truth behind it was hardly a secret the Varians kept; it was simply that no one was ever bothered enough to listen. Generation after generation, they pressed on, bound by the promise their ancestors had made in the ruins of Tiberia. When the [[Cruel Shattering]] struck in 100 AE, it was as unexpected as the first, and hit the fledgling Varian Empire as hard as the original shattering had hit Tiberia. They never fully recovered imperial status. Yet through every form their society took, their culture endured unchanged: militaristic, disciplined, and bound by a loyalty that outsiders found difficult to comprehend. By the [[Foretold Shattering]], some had begun to suspect a pattern. The [[Shattering of Revelation]] in 300 AE confirmed it. Once the centennial cycle was understood, the recurring disasters became something the world could brace for rather than merely survive. Communities rebuilt faster, people grew harder, and the average man learned to wield a blade as a matter of course. In this era a [[verdan-portal|portal]] to a new world was discovered, setting off a scramble among the nations. The great powers forced their passage through; others were turned away or chose to remain. Among the Varians, the divide was cultural. Those more open to the outside world may have crossed over, eventually leaving their Varian identity behind in [[Ayka]] when entering the new world of [[verdan|Verdan]]. The true believers, the custodians of the old promise, stayed. They watched with resentment as others departed, and drew closer together for it. Their numbers thinned, but their conviction hardened. When the [[Territorial Wars]] destroyed the portal and severed the connection between worlds, those left behind had to rebuild yet again — smaller now, and more isolated, but unbroken. - - - ### Culling (900) Main Article: [[Culling]] When the nations of the world were finally starting to heal, a new tragedy arose. The sky fell upon itself. The Culling brought no plague or monsters, but [[Spectral Reapers]] of every form and size. The night was a massacre, and the rivers ran red. Once more the world was torn asunder. Yet where other nations collapsed almost entirely, the Varians endured. Hardened by centuries of survival and unburdened by the sprawling infrastructure that made great powers vulnerable, they weathered the Culling where others could not. For the first time in generations, the horizon looked open. The promise made in the ruins of Tiberia was, perhaps, within reach once more. - - - ### Tenth Lude (900 ~ 1000) #### Valerian Era (900) **Emperor:** [[Publius Valerian]] **Notable Varians:** #### Council #### Octavian Era **Emperor:** [[Octavian]] **Notable Varians:** === --- ## Geography ### Provinces [[Aquilonia]] - Eastern Province [[Eldraea]] - Southern Ocean Temple Province [[Limani]] [[Praetoria]] [[Salopia]] - Northern Province [[Tiberia]] - Eastern Origin Province [[Old Vara]] [[Vara]] - Capital Province [[Venetiae]] - Southern Province === --- ## Culture ### Religion #### The Pelonian Pantheon Varians worship a religion closely related to that of the [[achaea|Achaeans]], drawn from the same ancient traditions. At the center of Pelonian faith sits the pantheon of deities said to live on [[Mount Pelion]], the highest mountain once part of Achaea. From their perch, they rule every aspect of human life. Pelonian gods and goddesses appear as men and women — though they can change themselves into animals and other forms — and are, as many myths recount, vulnerable to human foibles and passions. The traditional Achaean gods are highly diverse, each representing one aspect of nature or humanity. These range from gods of emotion to nature spirits whose existence is tied to a specific river or landmark. The Varian gods, however, differ markedly in character. Suited to a militaristic nation, they are far more martial in nature. The God of War holds the highest place in the Varian pantheon, rather than a god of the sea as in the Achaean tradition. The God of Wisdom concerns himself primarily with military tactics. The God of the Forge is known as the creator of weapons, not feats of engineering. In every aspect of the faith, one theme prevails: war. --- ### Customs #### Icy Greetings Varians drop cold water onto the head of any foreign visitor to their lands. This tradition is said to have started when a rival nation sent an unwashed tribesman to negotiate a peace treaty, acting as a deliberate slight against the Emperor. Varians never dropped the practice, and continue to douse foreigners to this day — fountains are installed at the entrances to cities to ensure it never fails. - - - #### Goddess of War Within the Tiberian capital stood the magnificent [[Temple of Beluna]], goddess of war. This site was the most important shrine in the Empire and was counted among the five wonders of the pre-shattering world. It was said to reach toward the heavens and to shine with light each day, built from the finest marble on the continent. The priests who give their lives to the goddess wounded their own arms or legs as a blood sacrifice, demonstrating their devotion. The temple carried additional political significance. To declare war on another state, a javelin was thrown from Tiberian territory into the temple, accompanied by a shout of 'Bellum!' — a symbolic act considered the formal opening of war. [[Beluna]], the Goddess of War, remains one of the most important deities in Varian culture, and some of the traditions mentioned still carry on. - - - #### Fantastical Festivals The Varian calendar is filled with festivals to the gods, and with them come celebrations. One of the best-known is Vestalia, consisting of a public banquet, private gift-giving, continuous feasting, and a carnival atmosphere that temporarily overturns Varian societal norms. It is best known as the day when slaves are treated as freedmen and permitted to wear the pilleus of a free citizen. In some cases, slaves are treated to a banquet and feast alongside their masters as equals. - - - #### Household Deities Each Varian family worships a specific god and prays to or honors their deity as a family each week. The chosen deity may change based on circumstance — a family that becomes fishermen or sailors may turn to the god of the sea. Before doing so, they must make a large offering to the god they are leaving, asking for forgiveness and giving thanks before moving on. --- ### Nomenclature Varian names are declarations of lineage, aspiration, and devotion. Drawing from the militaristic heritage of Tiberia, names emphasize virtues such as strength, honor, tactical wisdom, and social standing. References to gods, celestial bodies, and martial imagery are common, weaving religion and statecraft into personal identity from birth. Varians typically bear two to three names. The personal name, or praenomen, is given at birth and often reflects birth order — numerical names such as Quintus (fifth) or Septimus (seventh) are common, underscoring the ordered, hierarchical nature of Varian society. The family name, or cognomen, denotes clan affiliation or an inherited trait passed down through generations, and carries considerable social weight. Additional descriptors may be earned or granted later in life, reflecting deeds or qualities recognized by peers — Julius Sapienti, the wise, being one such example. There is a gendered pattern to naming. Male names tend to evoke leadership, valor, and endurance — Aurelius (golden), Magnus (great), Cornelius (horn, strength). Female names more often highlight grace, divinity, or familial harmony — Valeria (strong), Sperantia (hope), Aurelia (golden) — though strength is by no means absent from them. Neutral names blend these themes, often drawing from celestial or abstract concepts: Nox (night), Solidus (solid, firm), Oriana (dawn). --- ===
