Achaea

History

Ancient Past (... - 0)

Ask any Achaean where civilization began, and they will quickly reply that they, of course, created it. Achaean history stretches back thousands of years, during which they developed many practices that came to be seen as the pinnacle of civilization. Philosophy, medicine, and architecture were all revolutionized within the Achaean lands, achievements that have long been a source of pride for the people.

The peninsula that would become known as Achaea was home to numerous settlements that gradually coalesced into distinct political entities. The rugged coastline, fertile valleys, and access to maritime trade routes shaped the development of these early communities. Over generations, shared language, religious practices, and cultural traditions bound them together even as political rivalries kept them divided.

Age of the City-States

For much of their history, the Achaeans were divided into a multitude of small city-states, constantly vying with one another for land, wealth, and slaves. Although they shared a common culture and language, their political principles differed greatly. Some city-states, such as Leontinoi, relied on slavery and rule by a king, while others, like Pyrgos, practiced a form of democracy that even allowed slaves to vote.

Leontinoi and Pyrgos emerged as the two leading city-states, and they warred repeatedly for dominance over the peninsula. Each gathered coalitions of allied cities to support their cause. Pyrgos relied on its powerful navy to control trade routes and the seas, while Leontinoi used its disciplined army to dominate the land. This balance of power prevented either side from uniting the peninsula, leaving the Achaean people politically fragmented.

Achaean Colonies

Achaean colonies spread across the southern seas, stretching along much of the continent. These settlements adopted elements of both Achaean and local cultures, producing a mixture of languages and styles. Trade, migration, and local alliances shaped these colonies, allowing Achaean customs, laws, and religion to endure beyond the homeland.

The process of colonisation typically began with groups of settlers departing from established city-states, often driven by political exile, overpopulation, or the promise of wealth. Upon arrival, colonists would raid native populations for slaves and resources, or negotiate trading agreements. Over time, these outposts grew into prosperous cities that maintained cultural and commercial ties with the peninsula while developing distinct local identities.

Some colonies became particularly influential. They maintained trade networks with the peninsula, acted as hubs for resources, and occasionally intervened in the political affairs of Achaean city-states. The export of olive oil, wine, pottery, and metalwork from the homeland was exchanged for grain, timber, and luxury goods from distant shores. These colonies would later play a crucial role in Achaea's interactions with rising powers from the south, including Tiberia.

The Rise of Tiberia

Among the Achaean colonies, Tiberia became the most consequential. Founded by exiles from a minor Achaean city, it grew initially through raiding neighboring colonies and native settlements, acquiring slaves and resources essential for its survival. Located on a principal trade zone jutting into the southern seas, Tiberia prospered as traders stopped to exchange goods, food, and water, while the colony's control of slaves became a source of considerable wealth.

The expansion of Tiberia had significant repercussions for Achaea itself. Over time, Tiberia conquered nearby Achaean colonies and eventually extended its influence to include much of the Achaean homeland. Local cities faced demands for tribute, the stationing of garrisons, and interference in civic matters. Trade, military obligations, and political alliances were increasingly shaped by the presence of Tiberian authority.

Tiberian Rule over Achaea

Under Tiberian domination, Achaea was politically united for the first time in its history, but under foreign leadership. Foreign garrisons occupied cities, and Tiberian officials oversaw taxation, resource requisition, and the enforcement of imperial law. Achaean elites were affected by Tiberia's centralizing policies, which strengthened those with wealth and military connections. The wealthier grew stronger while traditional power structures were disrupted, though continued infighting and stagnant corruption prevented the emergence of a truly unified Achaean state that could rival the Tiberians.

Resentment toward Tiberian rule grew over time. The requisition of slaves, the killing of citizens, and the presence of foreign troops became sources of increasing discontent. Local autonomy was diminished as Tiberian governors imposed standardized policies across the peninsula. Yet many Achaean nobles found ways to prosper under the new order, collaborating with imperial authorities or securing positions within the administrative apparatus.

Despite these pressures, Achaeans retained their cultural identity. City councils continued to function, local laws persisted, and religious rites were observed. Citizens adapted to the presence of mercenaries, soldiers, and Tiberian administrators, navigating a system where survival and prosperity depended on aligning with imperial priorities. The Achaean language, customs, and philosophical traditions endured, taught in schools and practiced in homes across the peninsula.

Tiberia's military-driven expansion also influenced Achaean society. Local leaders and common citizens were often required to provide troops or resources for campaigns, yet the memory of independent city-states and the peninsula's cultural achievements endured. Even under foreign domination, Achaeans maintained pride in their history and heritage, keeping alive the ideals that had shaped their civilization for millennia. The dream of restored independence remained alive in the hearts of many, though few dared speak it openly.

Great Shattering (0 - 10)

The Achaeans were among the first peoples to perish during the great disasters and sicknesses of the Great Shattering, second only to the Tiberians. Their close ties through trade and government with Tiberian authorities hastened the spread of devastation. Earthquakes shattered cities, floods engulfed coastal settlements, and grotesque creatures once relegated to mythology emerged to terrorize the survivors. Within the first few months, much of the population was wiped out.

The social order that had existed for centuries collapsed in a matter of days. The wealthy watched helplessly as their expensive villas were stripped before their eyes and their farmlands scorched into cinders by an unforgiving sun. Many peasants who survived either perished in the chaos or disappeared entirely. Some returned to their former masters, driven by a lifetime of accumulated anger and resentment, exacting violent retribution. The civilization that had prided itself on philosophy, democracy, and architectural achievement descended into chaos that would have been unthinkable mere months before.

Those who survived the initial devastation faced relentless raids from barbarian groups, particularly from the northern tribes, who entered Achaea to loot and pillage what remained of its once-glorious lands. The barbarians conducted raid after raid, taking gold, food, and most importantly, slaves back to their homelands in triumph. Without organized defense or central authority, Achaean settlements fell one by one. The military traditions that had once produced disciplined armies capable of challenging their neighbors proved insufficient against enemies who struck without warning and vanished into territories now rendered unrecognizable by the reshaping of the land itself.

The feared Nasyrik plague stalked the land like a demon, deforming most survivors into a wretched race known as Mutes. These pitiful souls established small villages where they cowered from the nightly beasts that beset them. The lucky few who were not affected watched in horror as humanity was drained before their eyes. Many turned away from the gods, interpreting the calamities as divine punishment. This loss of faith led to a series of mass suicides that claimed much of the remaining population. Entire communities chose death over continued suffering, gathering in temples and sacred groves to end their lives together—a final, terrible testament to the depth of their despair.

Yet some Achaeans fled into the hills and mountains, escaping the worst of the disasters, sicknesses, and foreign invaders. Among these survivors was a fisherman named Charioss, who had been conscripted into Tiberian military service and separated from his wife Egonia Pelagius and young son Regulus on a small colony island. Life in the highlands was harsh, survival precarious, but the unbroken faith of refugees like Charioss allowed them to endure. They lived as their ancestors had in primitive times, hunting, foraging, and defending themselves from both human raiders and monstrous threats. These mountain communities maintained the old religious practices even as their coastal brethren abandoned the gods, preserving not just their lives but the spiritual traditions that had defined Achaean identity for millennia. After the land finally settled and the continent became divided, these survivors began returning to Achaea, prepared to rebuild what they believed to be the most civilised nation the world had ever known.

First Lude (10 ~ 100)

Age of Ikaros (10 - 28)

King: Ikaros

The Reunification Under Ikaros

When survivors came down from the mountains after the Shattering, they found their world unrecognizable. Coastlines had moved. Cities lay in ruins. The Achaean peninsula had fractured into scattered islands and broken territories. The wealthy families of Leontinoi and Pyrgos were dead or destitute. The slave economy had collapsed. Even the ancient rivalry between the two great city-states meant nothing now.

Charioss stepped into this vacuum. The conscripted fisherman had risen through Tiberian ranks during the chaos, and his background gave him an unusual advantage. Common survivors saw one of their own—a man who had cast nets and taught his son to fish. What remained of the aristocracy saw military experience and organizational skill. His survival credentials outweighed any pre-Shattering nobleman's claims.

But Charioss understood that reunifying Achaea required more than competence. It needed a symbolic break from the past. When he claimed leadership and proposed a unified kingdom instead of competing city-states, he took the name Ikaros. The transformation was deliberate. Charioss the fisherman, Charioss the conscript, Charioss who had been forced to abandon his family—all these identities stayed behind. Ikaros, first king of unified Achaea, embodied the rebirth itself.

The name change had personal motivations too. In Achaean culture, abandoning your family violated fundamental obligations, even under extreme circumstances like conscription and apocalypse. Friends were lifelong bonds. Breaking them invited ostracism. A king carrying that shame would undermine his own legitimacy. Whether his wife and son had survived remained unknown, but Ikaros buried the question with his old name.

Economic Strategy and Maritime Dominance

Ikaros' governance reflected both his origins and his training. He recognized that Achaea's traditional strengths—maritime culture, fishing expertise, strategic coastal position—could be leveraged in the new order. The kingdom pursued an unusual economic strategy: systematic coin hoarding. Achaea refused to spend or circulate currency beyond necessities, driving up the value of gold throughout the region. The policy pressured neighboring kingdoms while enriching Achaean coffers.

The Achaean people focused intensely on fishing, building on centuries of tradition. Their expertise gave them a near-monopoly on mending enhancements—specialized goods prized for repairing equipment in a world where craftsmanship remained scarce. Achaeans priced these enhancements high, concentrating more wealth within their borders. They accumulated quality fishing rods and aquatic treasures that proved valuable economically and diplomatically.

Ikaros never let economic focus compromise military readiness. He maintained a heavily militarized state with substantial weapons supplies, ensuring Achaea could defend its interests and project power when necessary. This combination positioned Achaea as an emerging power, punching above its weight despite a relatively small population.

Calisto, his second-in-command, helped consolidate royal authority while maintaining connections across different factions and regions.

Alliances, Betrayals, and the Question of Honor

In late spring of Year 10, Ikaros signed the First Treaty of Seurri with Prythonia. The alliance seemed natural—both nations valued military prowess, both had emerged from the Shattering with ambitions, and both faced potential threats from the Vara-Kurgan alliance. As a symbol of cooperation, Achaea and Prythonia established joint control over Noviodunum, a fortress on an island off the Prythonian coast, entrusted to the Senobryn family.

The alliance faced its first test when Calisto was captured by Conan and Ebbe of Kurgan in early summer. Ikaros chose to resolve it through personal combat rather than armies—traditional Achaean values. He lost the duel, exposing his limitations as a warrior. But Conan released Calisto unharmed, respecting Ikaros' courage. Despite their kingdoms' opposing alignments, the two men developed mutual respect. This later bore fruit in the Ikanan Mercenary Faction, a collaboration between Ikaros, Conan, and Auduin of Osterwalde that transcended borders.

When tensions between Prythonia and the Varan-Kurgan alliance intensified, Achaea profited from its ally's difficulties. Prythonia transferred full ownership of Noviodunum to Achaea. Ikaros responded by selling tridents to Prythonia at eighteen gold coins each—a price reflecting both Achaean market dominance and Prythonian desperation—and providing a fifty-gold-coin loan. The transactions showed Ikaros balancing alliance obligations with national interests.

The Prythonian alliance wasn't his only consideration. When Ikaros tried to fracture the Varan-Kurgan alliance by offering the Kurgans a counter-proposal, it failed. The incident was minor, quickly forgotten, but it showed his willingness to use cunning in statecraft. This approach peaked in late summer when he signed a secret treaty with Bastaernia, deciding their partnership offered more strategic value than continued alignment with Prythonia.

Prythonia's response was swift. When they discovered the Bastaernian treaty, they voided their alliance with Achaea. Achaeans were banned from Prythonian territory without permits. Prythonia demanded Noviodunum back. Ikaros complied, avoiding immediate conflict. But the Achaean nobility saw it differently. What the king viewed as justified alliance-building looked like treachery met with humiliation. The perceived mistreatment and forced fortress return demanded response.

On the ninth day of early autumn, Ikaros declared war on Prythonia. Four days later, the kingdoms clashed at the Battle of Tamaris—the first large-scale military engagement since the Shattering ended. But Ikaros himself didn't appear on the battlefield. Various explanations circulated: strategic reasoning, illness, political calculation. Some whispered a more personal cause: Regulus, Ikaros's long-lost son, had joined the Prythonian mercenaries. Whether the king knew this or simply feared the possibility remains unclear. The war with Prythonia intertwined with Ikaros' unresolved past in ways that complicated his leadership.

The Weight of the Past

The secret couldn't stay hidden forever. Regulus had survived the Shattering and eventually joined the Varian Empire, spending years searching for his father. The reunion, when it finally occurred through natural circumstances rather than deliberate seeking, brought neither shame nor judgment. Regulus understood the impossible circumstances—conscription, war, apocalypse—and reconciled. The meeting remained largely private, known to few beyond the royal circle, but it brought Ikaros some peace.

This personal resolution only set the stage for deeper tragedy. Regulus had become the leader and primary advocate of Eldraeanism, a new religion centered on dolphin deities. The faith opposed the Elyshi pantheon practiced primarily in Bastaernia, creating religious tensions that paralleled the political landscape. In Year 26, when Regulus was thirty-two, these tensions erupted into violence. Loki, a deity of the Elyshi faith, manifested and stabbed Regulus. Ikaros was present when his son fell.

Regulus' death didn't end the rivalry between Eldraeanism and the Elyshi faith. It made him a martyr. His sacrifice strengthened the Eldraean religion, attracting converts who saw proof of divine conflict. The religious war that followed pitted the Elyshi under Bastaernian protection against Achaea and Regulus' friends and allies throughout the known world. For Ikaros, the conflict brought together all the threads that had defined his reign: the Bastaernian alliance he'd pursued for strategic advantage now fractured under religious violence; the son he'd abandoned and then reconciled with became a symbol of faith; and the unified Achaea he'd built found itself defending not just territory or trade routes but the memory of the king's own family.

The Hollow Victory

The war Ikaros prosecuted was efficient and brutal. Achaea's military preparation, the economic strength built over decades, the maritime dominance—all of it came to bear. Elyshi temples burned. Sacred sites fell. Priests scattered into hiding. Bastaernia, caught off-guard by Achaean ferocity, lost ground steadily. By Year 28, Achaea held the upper hand. Victory seemed within reach.

But something changed in Ikaros as the war progressed. He grew distant, reflective. His orders remained sharp, his strategy sound, but those close to him noticed the long silences, the way he stared at reports of casualties without speaking. At some point—standing over a destroyed temple, or watching refugees flee, or seeing young soldiers who reminded him of Regulus—the king understood what he'd been refusing to see: none of this would bring his son back. It never could.

The war wasn't about faith. It wasn't about protecting Eldraeanism or punishing the Elyshi. It was grief wearing a crown, revenge dressed as righteousness. He'd been fighting to answer an impossible question: how does a father make meaning from his son's murder? The bodies piling up, Achaean and Bastaernian alike, provided no answer.

In late summer of Year 28, with his generals urging a final push that would crush Bastaernian resistance entirely, Ikaros instead ordered a halt. He sent word to Kaiser Auduin proposing terms. The generals were furious. The nobility was baffled. Achaea was winning. Why stop now?

The Treaty of Reconciliation, signed in early autumn, reflected neither victory nor defeat. Borders returned to pre-war positions. The Elyshi faith, nearly eradicated, was reduced to a marginal status, but was permitted to exist. No reparations were demanded by either side. No admission of fault. A mutual non-aggression pact. Both kingdoms walked away intact, neither having really won.

Bastaernia accepted the terms with a mixture of relief and confusion. They'd expected harsher conditions from a king who'd had them on the brink of collapse. Auduin, meeting Ikaros to sign the treaty, found his old friend changed—quieter, older, carrying a weight that had nothing to do with crowns or kingdoms. They spoke briefly of the years before the war, before Regulus's death, before everything had fractured. Then they signed their names and parted ways.

A Mysterious Departure

Three days after the treaty was signed, the royal council convened to find the king's study empty. On his desk lay a single letter, sealed with the royal mark. Inside, in Ikaros's hand, stood a name of someone known to the court but obscure, a minor official who had quietly opposed the war on practical and moral grounds. Someone with no power base, no allies in high places, who had never sought leadership. The letter named him successor of the throne.

There was no explanation. No farewell. No final proclamation.

A harbor watchman reported seeing someone matching the king's description boarding a small fishing boat in the hours before dawn. Alone. No guards, no retinue, no royal banners. Just a man and a boat. When called to, the figure didn't turn around. The boat was old, the kind used for day fishing along the coast—the kind Charioss might have used, decades ago.

By midday, the boat was gone. Some claimed they'd seen it heading north, others south. A few insisted it had gone east, toward open water. Weeks later, an empty fishing skiff washed ashore on the Achaean coast, battered but intact. Whether it was the same boat, no one could say for certain.

The fisherman who had become a king, who had transformed himself from Charioss into Ikaros, who had built a maritime power and navigated the treacherous waters of post-Shattering politics, had returned to the sea. Whether he'd found peace or oblivion, whether he lived out his days as an anonymous fisherman in some distant port or had let the waves claim him, no one ever knew.

Culture

Religion

The Pelion Pantheon

Central to Achaean mythology was the pantheon of deities said to live on Mount Pelion, the highest mountain in Achaea. From their perch, they ruled every aspect of human life. They resembled men and women, though they could change themselves into animals and other forms, and were vulnerable to human foibles and passions. As many myths recounted, the gods experienced jealousy, love, anger, and pride much as mortals did, making them relatable yet fearsome.

The Achaean gods were highly diverse, each representing one part of nature or human life. This ranged from emotions, such as anger, to nature spirits whose lifeforce was tied to a river or mountain. Some deities governed abstract concepts like wisdom or justice, while others presided over practical matters such as the harvest, warfare, or seafaring. Even under Tiberian rule, these deities continued to form the backbone of Achaean spiritual life, with temples and shrines maintained throughout the peninsula.

Worship involved regular offerings, sacrifices, and festivals dedicated to specific gods. Each city-state had patron deities who received particular veneration, and households maintained shrines to gods relevant to their occupations or circumstances. Oracles and priests served as intermediaries between mortals and the divine, interpreting signs and pronouncing the will of the gods on matters both personal and political.

Customs

Bathing

Achaeans traditionally bathed in the sea before embarking on any voyage, near or far, as a way to gain favour and luck from the god of the sea. This ritual purification was considered essential for safe passage, whether traveling to a neighboring island or crossing vast distances. The practice reflected the Achaeans' deep respect for maritime deities and their understanding of the sea's power over their fortunes.

They also washed each other the day before battle, symbolically cleansing fear and preparing for bravery. This communal washing served both practical and spiritual purposes, strengthening bonds between warriors while ensuring they entered combat free from doubt and terror. The ritual was believed to make soldiers bold in their fights ahead, allowing them to face death with courage.

Friendship and Loyalty

Friendships were not made lightly among the Achaean people. Making acquaintances and becoming good friends with another person was not something taken easily. Achaeans might seem standoffish at the beginning of a relationship, but warming behavior and increasing kindness indicated that trust was being built. Friends were seen as lifelong bonds, and breaking a friendship or violating trust between friends was viewed as highly dishonorable.

The one who broke such a bond would be seen as lesser in wider society and would be shunned until the friendship was renewed. This social ostracism could be devastating, affecting not just personal relationships but also business dealings, political alliances, and family standing. The importance placed on loyalty reinforced social cohesion and made Achaean communities tightly knit, bound together by webs of enduring personal obligations.

Nomenclature

Achaean names were strongly meaning-focused, with each name expressing a hoped-for quality rather than serving as a neutral identifier. Strength, wisdom, beauty, protection, and success recurred frequently—names like Aleksander (defender of man), Sofia (wisdom), and Nikephoros (bearer of victory) suggest that naming was a way of shaping or announcing a person's place in the world. Names functioned less as personal markers and more as compact statements of values.

The natural and divine worlds were central to this system. References to the sea, light, fire, and celestial bodies—Pelagios (the sea), Elektra (bright, shining), Pyrros (flame-like), Selene (moon)—reflect both the Achaeans' environment and their belief that human life unfolded under larger elemental and divine forces. Some names directly invoked divine favor, as with Mathias (gift of God), while others implied it through ideas of victory or flourishing, indicating an everyday integration of religion rather than overt ritual piety.

There was also a loose gendered pattern, though not without exception. Male names tended to emphasize conflict, endurance, or action—Theron (hunter), Tychon (hitting the mark)—while others carried darker or more ambiguous weight: Deimos (fear), Haemon (bloody), Orpheus (darkness of night). Female names more often highlighted beauty, growth, light, and harmony: Calisto (most beautiful), Efthalia (flourishing), Roxane (dawn), Zoe (life). Together, these patterns suggest a culture that understood identity as shaped by nature, the gods, and social expectation, with names serving as concise expressions of what a life was meant to embody.
