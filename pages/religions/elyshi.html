Elyshi

The Elyshi were a religious sect rooted in Aemocism, founded on the veneration of Loki above the broader pantheon. Where mainstream Aemocism distributed worship across its gods, the Elyshi held Loki as the primary divine figure and understood his nature as a mandate for expansion and the construction of sacred spaces across the world. The sect was international in reach, drawing members from many peoples, though the Kurgan accounted for the great majority of its following and gave it much of its character.

History

Founding

The Elyshi faith was founded on the first day of Late Summer, Year 10, by Kāre, a Kurgan, in the unsettled years that followed the Great Shattering. The timing was not incidental. Where the Shattering had torn apart existing order, Kāre's faith offered something that went beyond worship. The Elyshi were to expand, to build, and to leave behind them a world more beautiful and more ordered than the one they had inherited.

From its earliest days the faith drew followers from beyond Kurgan borders, its hierarchical structure and emphasis on collective construction offering an organisational clarity that many post-Shattering communities lacked. The Kurgan nonetheless remained its demographic heart. Roughly four in five Kurgan individuals counted themselves among its adherents — a proportion that reflected personal conviction rather than political mandate, as the faith never became the official religion of any kingdom.

Growth and Adoption

As the Elyshi expanded, its holy land was established at Aesam, southeast of Mori, the capital of Osterwalde. Aesam became the spiritual and administrative centre of the faith, housing its most sacred temples and the Chamber of Apostles. Temples spread outward from it as the sect grew, each understood not merely as a building but as an extension of the holy land itself as sanctified ground, and a physical expression of the Elyshi mandate to build.

The faith's most significant political development came when Kaiser Auduin of Bastaernia was raised to the rank of Apostle, the highest within the Elyshi hierarchy. Auduin did not impose the faith on his people, and the Elyshi never became a state religion of Bastaernia, but his personal commitment lent the sect considerable prestige and brought it under imperial protection. His closest advisor, Æthelstan, served as Head Priest, overseeing matters of doctrine and lore.

The Killing of Regulus and the Elyshi War

The crisis that would define and ultimately diminish the Elyshi came not from without but from the act of their own god. Regulus, son of Achaean king Ikaros, was the leading figure of Eldraeanism, a rival faith centred on dolphin deities, and the tension between the two religions had been growing for some time. It had not yet come to open conflict when, in Year 26, Loki manifested and killed him.

The act was irrevocable in its consequences. For the Elyshi, it was a moment of terrible ambiguity: their god had intervened directly in the world and got rid of the opposition, but in a manner that would bring ruin upon them. For Ikaros, it was the murder of his son. Regulus became a martyr, his death the founding wound of a faith strengthened by sacrifice, and the cause of a war the Elyshi had not sought and could not survive.

Ikaros led Achaea against Bastaernia, and the Elyshi, sheltering under Bastaernian protection, bore much of the destruction. Sacred sites fell. Temples burned. The sect's clergy scattered into hiding. By Year 28, when Auduin and Ikaros signed the Treaty of Reconciliation, the Elyshi had been gravely diminished.

Aftermath

The Treaty of Reconciliation ended the war but offered the Elyshi nothing. Bastaernia withdrew its protection of the sect under the treaty's terms and ceased to recognise it as a legitimate offshoot of Aemocism. What had once sheltered under imperial backing and grown in its shadow was now left to sustain itself on whatever it could hold together.

Doctrine

The Nature of Loki

The Elyshi understood Loki not merely as one god among many but as the most active and present of the divine. Where other gods rested or withdrew from the world, Loki remained wandering all seven worlds, giving humanity the runic alphabet, carving rivers and grinding mountains with wind. Some within the sect held that he had given humanity fire as well. His nature resisted simple definition: knowledge, chaos, and transformation were all bound up in him, and the Elyshi made no attempt to resolve these into a neat image. They venerated the whole.

The broader Aemocist pantheon was acknowledged. Loki's primacy within it was not negotiable.

Expansion and Construction

The Elyshi mandate was twofold: to grow and to build. New members were actively sought, and the construction of sacred architecture was understood as devotion made physical. Altars, temples, and grand temples formed a hierarchy of sacred spaces, each requiring greater collective effort than the last. Grand temples — the most ambitious undertakings of the faith — were dedicated to specific gods and required the approval of the high council before work could begin. Each new structure was an assertion of Elyshi presence in the world, and the faith measured its own health partly in what it had built.

Organisation

The Elyshi were governed through a hierarchy of five ranks, each carrying distinct obligations and privileges.

Disciples

Formed the broad base of the faith. Their obligations were minimal: to abstain from the worship of other gods and to answer the summons of senior clergy when called for religious wars or grand ceremonies.

Priests

Took on active roles in the recruitment of new members and the construction of altars. They participated in holy wars and were recognised by a customised ceremonial staff.

High Priests

Oversaw the construction of temples and held seats on the high council, where they could raise matters of doctrine and policy. Elevation to this rank was marked by ceremony, and came with an upgraded staff.

Head Priests

Bore genuine administrative responsibility, overseeing the construction of grand temples, chairing the weekly meetings of the high council, and serving as the senior governing body of the faith. Their elevation was celebrated at a grand temple, and they were distinguished by robes and high-grade ceremonial regalia. Any ruler who brought the Elyshi under their personal protection was accorded this rank.

Apostles

Stood at the summit of the hierarchy, distinguished by personal ceremony, a permanent commemorative place in the Chamber of Apostles, and a unique title. Elevation was not granted lightly, requiring demonstrated service across architectural, evangelical, and administrative domains. Apostles held authority over the elevation of High Priests and served as the ultimate governors of the faith.

Holy Land

The Elyshi holy land, known as Aesam, lay southeast of Mori, the capital of Osterwalde. It was the spiritual heart of the sect: the site of its most sacred temples, the seat of its high council, and the home of the Chamber of Apostles. Access was strictly controlled. Non-members could enter only under the direct supervision of a High Priest or above, and the inner gardens were reserved for senior clergy alone. Temples elsewhere in the world were regarded as extensions of Aesam, carrying the same sanctity and the same protections.
