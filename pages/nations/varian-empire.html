Varian Empire

History

Tiberia (... - 0)

The origins of what would become the Varian Empire lie in the distant past, in a minor Achaean colony founded by exiles seeking power in a new land. These exiles, departing from a minor Achaean city, established their settlement through raiding the natives and neighboring colonies, acquiring goods and slaves essential to starting their new colony.

Over time, this colony grew into a city strategically located on a principal trade zone jutting out into the southern seas. The settlement came onto the map as traders routinely stopped there to trade goods and take on food and water. But their principal commodity of value was slaves. The city leaders continued to conquer the natives, taking the much-needed slaves which they could then sell to traders, and soon the city grew wealthy.

The Rise of Tiberian

Along with newfound wealth came a rise in ambition. The city was wracked with internal struggles as inhabitants used their money to try and gain power, causing a power vacuum as the great houses fought between one another, killing off their opposition. Finally, a great general known as Tiberian seized power, expelling the landed houses of the city and taking their riches. He was quick to make use of this opportunity, using this money to buy mercenaries and taking over the surrounding Achaean colonies, letting them choose between subjugation or having the city burnt to the ground. Before long, the entire peninsula was his, and he crowned himself as the leader of a new nation: the Empire of Tiberia.

Little of its formation is known, other than that their point of origin was a long-dead colony. The mighty general known as Tiberian repurposed the abandoned town, and used it as a starting point for his and his army’s campaign across the known world. Tiberian conquered neighbouring cities with ease, and grew a prospering empire known as Tiberia. Tiberia, despite the impressive military it bolstered, often had little use for it. Due to its well structured government and central location, people came to them. Towns would offer themselves to the growing nation for its protection and just governing so that they too could revel in the life Tiberia gave.

The Tiberian Military State

Tiberia was set apart from other Achaean colonies by its rigid military tradition. The best way to rise through the ranks was to serve in the army and win glory against the surrounding natives and colonies. The only way to survive in the city was to win battles, and Tiberia spent the next century taking over as much as they could, subjugating all of the Achaean people and the natives who lived upon the southern sea coast.

The Tiberian Legion was once considered the most fearsome military in the world. Consisting of a meticulously organised and disciplined force divided by the decimal, using fearsome tactics and superior equipment, they managed to conquer much of the continent in a matter of centuries. Citizens of the Tiberian Empire are drafted to serve a minimum of 10 years of service, followed by a pension and land grant. This system encouraged military service for the poor, and meant that they had no lack of recruits. Of course, this system drove on the war machine by needing an ever growing amount of land to hand out, meaning that the Tiberian Empire had to continue growing to support the system. This military tradition lived on past the Shattering, and each Varian citizen was educated in the art of war.

Soon, the city of Tiberia became the new center of the continent, with traders from across the world flocking there to trade lucrative goods from across the Empire. The Imperial Government was centered there, and the military would parade there whenever they won a triumph against a foreign foe.

The Tiberian Empire was ever growing and ever successful for hundreds of years. Unfortunately, the old addich rang true: The larger they are, the harder they fall. In a great disaster that swept the land, almost the entirety of the Tiberian Empire was destroyed in a matter of days.

Great Shattering (0 - 10)

The Tiberians were the ones worst affected by the Shattering. Many of the Tiberian cities and colonies across the world were wiped out instantly by divine disasters brought on by the gods' wrath, causing cities to shake and break apart, great winds strong enough to overturn buildings, and great waves as tall as mountains hitting the coasts. Not even the royal family were spared, and the direct descendants of Tiberian himself perished in the events.

Among those who survived was Aurelius, second son of Septimus Vara, head of one of the most powerful families in the old Empire. Born into privilege but treated as a spare rather than an heir, Aurelius had been banished to a military academy on the mountainous northern border following his discovery sneaking out at night to engage in petty theft with street children. The academy's harsh discipline had forged him into an exceptional student and warrior, though he harbored intense resentment toward his family.

When he reached his name day and returned to Vara to choose his patron deity, Aurelius scaled the high staircase to the Temple of Beluna, goddess of war. He knelt before her altar and sealed his devotion by marking his face with a knife, letting blood flow past his eyes without flinching. The goddess's blessing altered his destiny forever.

A few nights later, the world began to shake. Aurelius fled the academy with a few younger boys, hiding in the woods and surviving on what they could find. When they finally emerged a month later, they found only ruins. Every town they reached was empty of life, with only gangs of bandits appearing to loot the survivors. Even the great city of Vara was reduced to a shadow of its former glory.

Only a few Tiberians survived, those from the city of Vara having left the cities and entered the mountains before the worst of the Shattering occurred. It was far from the lush farmlands and trade centers that they had lived in before, but they managed to continue on despite the desolate area.

The social system of the civilized world that had been in place for centuries had been broken down in a matter of days. Those that had once been rich had their expensive villas stripped before their eyes, their vast swathes of farmland being scorched into cinders by the sun, and their peasants either killed or disappearing. Many of those peasants came back to their old owners, taking out a lifetime of anger and resentment. Soon, the only way to survive was to go back to their primative roots, resorting to the killing, raping and looting that reserved for those deemed uncivilised.

The barbaric tribes of the north were the ones who benefited most from this breakdown of the natural order. Their lands were far from the centre of the disaster, and their warlike societies were well suited to this new world order, allowing them to quickly take advantage of the issues in the south. They conducted raid after raid into the new broken lands, taking gold, food and slaves back to their homelands in triumph. For a while the barbarian tribes grew into vastly wealthy kingdoms, until their hubris got the better of them.

For pestilence haunted all survivors - civilised or uncivilised. Like a demon the feared Nasyrik plague stalked the lands. It deformed most into a wretched race to be known as “mutes”. These pitiful souls established small villages where they cowered from the nightly beasts that beset them. The lucky few who were not affected watched in horror as humanity was drained before their eyes.

Aurelius turned his back to the lands of the past and returned to the mountains, collecting survivors along the way. The land was harsh, and survival difficult, but they managed to continue. His leadership saved the lives of many, and he was soon elected as the first Emperor of what remained of the Varian people. Thankfully, due to Vara's isolation in the mountains, they escaped the worst of the Nasyrik pandemic that plagued the lands over the following years.

The most prominent Tiberian state to survive was that of Vara. The mighty city withstood the ravishing of the land, and vowed to rebuild their Empire to last all of eternity. Now the last Tiberian citizens left in the continent, the people strove to reclaim what was once theirs and to re-establish the Tiberian Empire in a new age, now reborn like a Phoenix as the Varian Empire.

First Lude (10 ~ 100)

Aurelian Era (? - 10)

Emperor: Aurelius Vara

Aurelius led the Varian people from the highlands, bringing with him survivors of the greatest catastrophe the world had known. Under his rule, the Varian Empire began its long struggle to reclaim what was once theirs and re-establish their prominence in a shattered world. The military traditions of Tiberia lived on, with each Varian citizen educated in the art of war, maintaining the disciplined approach that had once made the Tiberian Legion the most feared force on the continent.

The city of Vara, having withstood the ravages of the Shattering due to its mountainous location, served as the first capital of the reborn empire. From this defensible position, Aurelius worked to establish the foundations of Varian power. His devotion to Beluna remained absolute, and the goddess's blessing seemed to guide his decisions as he navigated the treacherous politics of the post-Shattering world.

On the 2nd of Early Summer, 10 AE, Aurelius stepped down from the imperial throne and appointed Julius sapienti as his successor. The reasons for his abdication remained largely private, though some speculated that the burdens of rebuilding an empire from ruins had taken their toll. Regardless, his transition of power was peaceful and orderly, reflecting the Varian commitment to stability and proper succession.

Julian Era (10 - ?)

Emperor: Julius sapienti ex Vara

Julius sapienti inherited an empire still finding its footing in the new world. Where Aurelius had been a warrior blessed by the goddess of war, Julius brought a different approach to leadership. His cognomen "sapienti" - the wise - spoke to his reputation for careful thought and strategic planning. Under his rule, the Varian Empire continued its efforts to expand influence and reclaim the glory of Tiberia, though the challenges remained immense.

The empire Julius inherited was small but disciplined, its people hardened by survival in the mountains and united by their shared Tiberian heritage. The military tradition remained strong, with citizens still required to serve and train in the arts of war. Yet the world had changed fundamentally, and the new emperor would need to navigate alliances, threats, and opportunities that Tiberian rulers of old had never imagined.

Ludes (100 ~ 900)

Centuries of Struggle

Historically, the Varians were a faction many considered an upstart, a band of fools playing as a nation. A small group tried to stake their claim and build the great Varian Empire for countless generations, though rarely did they ever grow powerful enough to pose a threat to any established nation. They tried their hand at being seafaring nomads, cave dwellers, and everything in between, to no avail.

Many were confused by the Varians' undying loyalty to their historic hope, not understanding why so many generations continued to try to establish a nation that failed time and time again, always pushed back to the brink by those in power. The truth was hardly a secret the Varians kept; it was simply that no one was bothered enough to hear their story.

The Varian Empire stood tall for countless years as one of the strongest powers in the world before eventually being buried under the tides of time. Ever since, the most loyal to the Varian Empire remained generation by generation, trying to rebuild their great nation in order to complete the promise made by their ancestors so many years ago.

As many had privately feared, the denizens of the world could not hold the peace. A hundred years after the Shattering, and each and every century that followed, the Shattering would rear its head once again to inflict pain and destruction. As time passed the people grew less and less afraid. Not only could they rebuild with ease, but also prepare for the recurring event. Adjusting to the undead and other horrors stalking the lands, the average person grew more fit and able. Many had learned to wield a blade in combat.

In the wilds of the world, away from the now thriving civilizations, a band of explorers stumbled across a unique structure. Upon return, they boasted of their discovery to all who would listen. Few believed the claims of the men. Who could believe something as far-fetched as a portal to a new world? Nearing a year of the adventurer’s boasts, they finally convinced one to listen: a researcher.

The researcher was a distinguished scholar, said to be of royal blood from times long forgotten. Upon seeing the portal for himself, he shared the findings with his peers. In the following year, nations from every corner of the world became aware of it. This was not a widely known fact, but among the ranks of nobility, the thought became worrisome. In order to fuel their nations, they mined and harvested all that was available. With wild excitement, the nation leaders led their people to the new world beyond the portal’s gate. While the largest of empires forced their passage, many were denied, left behind, or simply did not wish to journey within. During the following territorial war, the portal was destroyed and the connection between the worlds severed. Those who remained in their homeworld were left to pick up the pieces. With the communities small and the most skilled gone, the centennial Shatterings crippled who were left.

Culling (900)

When the nations of the world were finally starting to heal, a new tragedy arose. The sky fell upon itself… The Culling did not bring forth new monsters or plagues, but spectral reapers of all forms and sizes. The night was a massacre, and the rivers ran red with the blood of the innocent. Once more the nations were torn asunder. From the rubble, the survivors would have to rebuild what was once again lost.

In the disaster of the Culling, the great powers of the world were brought to their knees, crippled and ravaged just like Tiberia before them. Following in their predecessors' footsteps, the Varian Empire had to rise from tragedy once more.

Religion

The Pelonian Pantheon

The Tiberians worshipped a religion very similar to that of the Achaeans, being based off the same traditions. At the center of Pelonian was the pantheon of deities who were said to live on Mount Pelion, the highest mountain in Achaea. From their perch, they ruled every aspect of human life. Pelonian gods and goddesses looked like men and women (though they could change themselves into animals and other things) and were—as many myths recounted—vulnerable to human foibles and passions.

The traditional Achaean gods were highly diverse, each of them representing one part of nature or humans. This could vary from being an emotion, like a god of anger, to a nature spirit whose lifeforce was tied to a certain river.

However, the Tiberian gods were very different in style. The gods were much more militarized to match the centralized nation. The God of War was the highest god in the Tiberian pantheon, rather than a god of the sea as in the Achaean one. The God of Wisdom was primarily a god of Military Tactics. The god of the forge was known as the creator of weapons, rather than feats of engineering. In these examples, one can see that the gods were suited to one thing, and one thing only: War.

Customs

Icy Greetings

The Tiberians had a custom whereby they dropped cold water onto the head of any foreign visitor to their lands. It was said that this strange tradition started when a rival nation sent an unwashed tribesman to negotiate a peace treaty unsuccessfully, acting as a slight against the Emperor. As such, the Tiberians never dropped the act and continued to douse foreigners ever since, even installing fountains at the entrances to cities so that it always followed.

Goddess of War

Within the capital of the Tiberian Empire was the magnificent Temple of Beluna, the goddess of war. This site was the most important shrine within the Empire and as such was known as one of the 5 Wonders of the Pre-Shattering world. It was said to have reached to the heavens and to have shone with light each day, being built from the finest marble on the continent. The priests who gave their lives to the goddess wounded their own arms or legs as a blood sacrifice to show their devotion to the deity.

The Temple had an additional political importance within Tiberian society. To declare war on another state, a javelin was thrown from Tiberian territory into the temple, accompanied by a shout of 'Bellum!', as a symbolic act considered as the opening of war.

Fantastical Festivals

The Tiberian calendar was filled with festivals to the gods, and with them came celebrations. One of the best-known festivals was that of Vestalia, consisting of a public banquet, private gift-giving, continual partying, and a carnival atmosphere that overturned Tiberian societal norms. It was best known for being the day where slaves were treated as freedmen and allowed to wear the 'pilleus' of a free citizen. In some cases, the slaves were treated to a banquet and even feasted together with their masters as equals.

Household Deities

Each family worshipped a specific god and would pray or honor their god as a family each week. The deity of each family may change based on environmental changes; they may pray to the god of the sea if the family becomes fishermen or sailors. They must make a large offering to the god they are leaving, however—this is to ask for forgiveness and thanks before moving on.

Nomenclature

Varian names draw heavily from the militaristic and imperial heritage of the Tiberian tradition, often deriving from Latin roots that emphasize virtues like strength, honor, wisdom in warfare, and social status. Unlike mere identifiers, names serve as declarations of aspiration or lineage, reflecting the empire's focus on hierarchy, conquest, and resilience. Numerical names (e.g., those indicating birth order) are common, underscoring the structured, orderly society. References to gods, nature, light, and elements of power invoke divine favor or martial prowess, integrating religion and statecraft into personal identity.

Family names (cognomina) are essential, denoting clan affiliations or inherited traits, and individuals typically bear 1-2 personal names (praenomina or additional descriptors) alongside them. There is a gendered pattern: male names frequently evoke leadership, valor, endurance, or tactical acumen, aligning with the military ethos; female names more often highlight grace, prosperity, divinity, or familial harmony, though both can carry connotations of strength. Neutral names blend these themes, often abstract or celestial, allowing flexibility in a society valuing adaptability amid conquest.
