# Suaring ## About Suaring was one of two dominant Bastaerni kingdoms to emerge from the [[Great Shattering]], and the older rival of [[Osterwalde]] with whom it would eventually merge to form the [[Bastaernian Empire]]. The Suari traced their identity to the legendary [[King Suara]], whose triumph over a river god through endurance and cunning established the values that would define their culture for generations: persistence over brute force, adaptability over rigidity, and mastery of waterways as both livelihood and weapon. Like all Bastaerni peoples, the Suari had spent most of their pre-shattering history locked in cycles of internal raiding and feuding, their ancient blood rivalry with the Osterwaldi as central to their identity as any founding myth. The Great Shattering devastated the old tribal order and pushed the Suari through a period of displacement and survival before they re-established themselves along the northern coasts. Under [[mara-of-suaring|Queen Mara]], the kingdom reached the height of its political significance—and its formal end. Her agreement with [[auduin|King Auduin]] of Osterwalde to dissolve both crowns and found the Bastaernian Empire was the first peaceful union between the two peoples in recorded history. The Suari did not vanish into that empire so much as diverge: some remained and were absorbed into the new imperial structure, while others departed quietly with their queen toward distant horizons, carrying Suari culture with them into a journey that would outlast Mara herself by centuries. === --- ## History ### Ancient Tribes (... - 0) The Suari traced their origins to the legendary [[King Suara]], who claimed descent from the god of rivers. Suara was remembered as both the founder of his people and as a challenger of the divine. According to myth, Suara boasted of being the greatest swimmer, the one who could hold his breath the longest, and the best fisherman in the world. The local river god, who took the form of a turtle, became offended by this immense pride and challenged Suara to a contest. The god wagered that if Suara won, he would be given the finest woman in the world, but if Suara lost, he would drown in the river. The two fought hard across a series of trials that lasted for weeks, testing swimming ability, breath-holding endurance, and fishing skill. In the end, Suara emerged victorious, defeating the river god and securing both his survival and his legendary bride. From this triumph, the Suaring clan claimed its origin and derived its identity as a people favoured by cunning and perseverance over divine power. The Suari were part of the broader Bastaerni peoples, a highly tribalised society that inhabited the vast forest lands of the south. The Bastaerni were divided into hundreds of petty kings, each ruling a tribe or even a subdivision of one. This highly decentralised structure led to frequent conflicts between tribes, and when combined with a strongly warlike culture, resulted in near-constant internal warfare. The Suari participated fully in this cycle of raiding and feuding, leaving their territory primarily to trade with foreign peoples or to conduct raids against those who strayed too close to the forest borders. Throughout their early history, the Suari maintained a blood rivalry with the [[Osterwalde]] people, another major Bastaerni tribe. This enmity stretched back to time immemorial, rooted in the mythical conflict between the two tribes' legendary founders—Suara and Walde. The rivalry shaped Suari identity and military focus for generations, ensuring that much of their martial tradition was honed in conflicts with their closest cultural relatives rather than distant foreign powers. On several occasions throughout history, strong leaders managed to unite the scattered Bastaerni tribes, usually in response to large-scale foreign invasions. These rare unifiers were remembered as harsh and uncompromising rulers, willing to employ brutality to impose unity. The Suari participated in some of these temporary confederations, but such periods of consolidation never lasted longer than a single generation. Upon the death of the unifying king, tribal autonomy reasserted itself and internal conflict resumed. As northern foreigners, particularly the [[achaea|Achaeans]] and [[varian-empire|Tiberians]], began establishing colonies along the northern coast, the Suari developed an ambivalent relationship with them. At times, they raided colonial forts and settlements; at others, they engaged in trade. Through this exchange, the Suari entered wider continental networks, exporting furs in return for luxury goods such as silks, dyes, and metals. The Achaeans and Tiberians frequently attempted to manipulate Bastaernian politics by financing and arming rival tribes, deliberately exacerbating internal divisions. Despite these efforts, neither power succeeded in conquering the southern forests, and the Suari maintained their independence throughout the pre-imperial period. - - - ### Great Shattering (0 - 10) Main Article: [[Great Shattering]] The Great Shattering reached the Suari and other Bastaerni peoples later than most continental societies, largely due to their geographic isolation and limited integration into imperial systems. When it did arrive, its effects were devastating. The Suari possessed little resistance to the [[Nasyrik Plague]] that had long circulated through the Tiberian Empire and surrounding regions. Entire villages and clans were wiped out as disease spread rapidly through coastal settlements and inland routes. Mass flight followed, as Suari survivors moved southward into the Bastaerni heartlands. This displacement often worsened the crisis, unintentionally spreading infection further into previously unaffected regions. In response, several Suaring chieftains implemented harsh but effective containment measures. These typically involved the execution of the infected and the strict isolation of corpses from the wider population. Through such measures, a small number of leaders survived the shattering and absorbed neighbouring tribes and clans that had lost their leadership entirely. These surviving Suari remained in the southern regions for many years, until the plagues subsided and populations developed partial immunity. The cost of survival, however, was immense. The northern forests that had been the Suari homeland had been largely destroyed by the divine disasters associated with the shattering. Climatic changes eventually rendered the southern lands uninhabitable, forcing the surviving Suari back north into territories now largely devoid of human life. From this upheaval emerged the Suari kingdom, one of two dominant Bastaerni successor states alongside their ancient rivals, the Osterwaldi. Both settled along the northern coasts and increasingly turned their attention outward, carrying their ancestral rivalry into a new geopolitical context. - - - ### First Lude (10 ~ 100) The Suari emerged as one of the most powerful Bastaernian kingdoms in the post-shattering world. In the post-shattering period, the Suari kingdom became known for adaptability and cunning. Its rulers exploited river networks for trade and raiding, drawing on the legendary heritage of Suara's mastery of waterways and aquatic skills. The kingdom maintained a long-standing blood feud with the Osterwaldi, their closest neighbours and oldest enemies. The kingdom maintained the traditional Bastaerni warrior ethos. Education remained rare, with most Suari born into working families where they learned trades rather than formal learning. Those with greater wealth received basic education, but it remained limited in comparison with other nations. Instead, the Suari made up for lack of formal education through physical prowess and martial training, learning fighting and hard work above all else. The legendary heritage of king Suara and the divine favour claimed through descent from the river god remained central to Suari political legitimacy and cultural identity. The story of Suara's triumph over the turtle-formed deity through weeks of grueling competition served as a cultural touchstone, emphasising the values of persistence, skill, and the ability to overcome seemingly insurmountable challenges through determination rather than brute force alone. #### Under Mara (? - 10) **Queen:** [[mara-of-suaring|Mara]] ##### Unification with Osterwalde Following the [[Varian–Prythonian War]], [[auduin|King Auduin]] of Osterwalde pursued an unprecedented diplomatic initiative toward the Suari kingdom. Rather than continuing the ancient blood feud through military conquest, Auduin and his advisor Æthelstan proposed a peaceful union. Negotiations culminated in a historic meeting between King Auduin and Queen Mara of the Suari. The two rulers agreed to dissolve their respective crowns and establish an entirely new political entity: the Bastaernian Empire. This decision represented the first time in recorded history that the Suari and Osterwaldi had peacefully joined rather than fought. For the Suari, this meant abandoning centuries of enmity rooted in the mythical conflict between Suara and [[king-walde|Walde]], a profound cultural transformation that required Queen Mara to persuade her people that unity offered greater advantages than continued rivalry. During the unification ceremony, the earth god [[Aemok]] was said to have appeared, enraged by the peaceful joining of rival peoples and demanding blood sacrifice to sanctify the new order. [[aethelstan-halfhand|Æthelstan]] stepped forward to face the god, expressing both regret and satisfaction at the fulfilment of his vision for Bastaernian unity. When Aemok reached for his divine axe, it was found missing—Auduin had taken it. The king struck Æthelstan with the weapon, severing his advisor's right hand rather than killing him. Auduin declared that this sacrifice would be the only blood spilled for Bastaernia. Auduin was proclaimed Kaiser of the [[Bastaernian Empire]], and the Suari transformed from an independent kingdom into a constituent part of a larger imperial structure. Queen Mara's role in the unified empire and the political arrangements that preserved Suari autonomy ensured that the union was more than simple conquest, though the Osterwaldi remained the dominant force in the new polity. #### Mara's Departure Main Article: [[Mara]] Among Mara's entourage were those who had heard of [[Quart]]—a continent so far removed from the world's troubles that the Shatterings had never touched it. Few in [[Ayka]] knew it existed at all. Mara did, and when her work in Suaring was done, she set her sights on it. She handed leadership to Kaiser Auduin and departed with a part of her people—not quietly, not loudly, but with the ease of someone who had already made up her mind long before anyone else noticed. There was no grand announcement, no ceremony to match the one that had just unified Bastaernia. She simply left, and the world moved on around the space she left behind. The voyage she began that day would stretch across centuries of pilgrimage before it reached its end. The nation that eventually rose from it bears her name still: [[Mara]]. **Notable Suari:**
#### The Bastaernian Empire Main Article: [[Bastaernian Empire]] Under Auduin's rule as Kaiser, elements of foreign administrative practice were combined with traditional Suari and Bastaerni tribal values. The empire was divided into semi-autonomous states, each governed by its own leader, preserving local traditions and leadership patterns within a broader imperial framework. The Suari presence within the empire remained strong—the formidable [[Sigfrid]] and others held their ground, ensuring that Suaring did not simply dissolve into Osterwalde's shadow. Their expertise in riverine trade and waterway navigation continued to shape imperial economic life, and the ancient rivalry with the Osterwaldi gradually gave way to something more cooperative under the unifying hand of Auduin and Æthelstan. === --- ## Geography ### Settlements [[Middle]] === --- ## Culture ### Religion #### Aemocism Main Article: [[Aemocism]] The Suari practiced Aemocism, the traditional Bastaerni religion. Most of the surviving mythology centred on the plights of the gods and their interaction with various other beings, such as humanity and the jötnar, beings who might be friends, lovers, foes, or family members of the gods. The cosmos in Bastaerni mythology consisted of Seven Worlds that flanked a central tree, Læraðr. Units of time and elements of the cosmology were personified as deities or beings. While the Suari shared the broader Bastaerni pantheon with the Osterwaldi and other tribes, their particular devotion to the god of rivers reflected their legendary founder's heritage. The story of [[king-suara|Suara]]'s contest with the river god in turtle form remained central to Suari religious practice, emphasising themes of human cunning and perseverance against divine power. Other significant deities included [[Aemok]], the battle-axe-wielding, humanity-protecting earth god who relentlessly fought his foes; his son [[Loki]], who craftily pursued knowledge throughout the worlds and bestowed among humanity the runic alphabet; the beautiful, feathered cloak-clad goddess who rode to battle to choose among the slain; the vengeful hunting goddess who preferred the wolf howls of the winter mountains to the seashore; the god whose weather and farming associations brought peace and pleasure to humanity; and the widowed goddess who kept apples that granted eternal youthfulness. --- ### Customs #### Warrior Ethos Education remained rare among the Suari people. Most were born into poor, working families and learned trades rather than formal education. This differed for those with greater wealth, but even they received only basic education in comparison with other nations. Instead of formal learning, most Suari compensated through physical strength and combat training, learning fighting and hard work above all else. This emphasis on martial prowess over scholarship reflected the Suari's forest-dwelling heritage and the constant tribal warfare that had characterised their pre-Shattering existence. Even after unification into the Bastaernian Empire, the Suari maintained this traditional value system, producing skilled warriors and craftsmen rather than scholars or administrators. Their legendary founder's reputation for physical excellence—swimming ability, breath-holding endurance, and fishing skill—continued to shape cultural ideals of what constituted worthy achievement. --- ### Nomenclature Suari names reflected the tribal, nature-bound heritage of the people, rooted in Old Germanic and Norse influences that evoked strength, kinship with the land, valor in battle, and ties to the divine and mythical. They carried aspirations for endurance, wisdom, or prowess—Tancred (thought counsel), Eadred (wealthy counsel), Sigfrid (victory peace)—mirroring a society where survival and honor were paramount. References to animals, elements, and ancestral deeds underscored their insular, warlike culture, with wolf imagery appearing frequently enough to suggest particular cultural weight: Actulf (noble wolf), Coenwulf (bold wolf), Wulfdag (wolf day), Wulfstan (wolf stone). Individuals often bore a single given name, sometimes augmented by a clan descriptor or epithet, emphasising tribal loyalty. Male names leaned toward themes of protection, leadership, and ferocity — Heinrich (home ruler), Leofric (beloved ruler), Ekkehart (hard edge)—aligning with the demands of raiding and defence. Female names more frequently suggested grace, resilience, or natural harmony: Ælfwynn (elf joy), Brihtwyn (bright joy), Ermintrude (universal strength), Ealhswith (temple strength). Both nonetheless shared connotations of unyielding spirit, and the line between them was one of emphasis rather than kind. --- ===
