# Bastaernian Empire ## About The Bastaernian Empire was a unified state forged from the forests and coastal territories of the southern continent of Ayka, built upon centuries of tribal tradition and internal conflict. For most of their history, the Bastaerni had resisted unification, their society divided among hundreds of petty kings whose warlike culture kept them locked in near-constant internal warfare. It was only in the aftermath of the Great Shattering, which devastated the old tribal order and forced the survivors north into depopulated lands, that conditions emerged for something new. Two ancient rivals—the Osterwaldi and the Suari—eventually set aside their blood feud and dissolved their respective kingdoms to form a single empire, the first and only unified Bastaernian state. Under Kaiser Auduin, the empire combined traditional tribal values with elements of foreign administrative practice, establishing a structure of semi-autonomous states that secured long-term stability without erasing the regional identities that had defined Bastaernian life for generations. The fate of the empire remains uncertain, though most accounts suggest it fell victim to the Cruel Shattering—an event as catastrophic as the Great Shattering a century before. What cultural continuity survived departed before that end. Queen Mara of the Suari led her people on a great pilgrimage to the continent of Quart, carrying Bastaernian traditions with her into a world far removed from the southern forests. A nation there would be founded in her name: Mara. === --- ## History ### Ancient Tribes (... - 0) The Bastaerni were a highly tribalised society, illustrating both the strengths and weaknesses of extreme decentralisation. They inhabited the vast forest lands of the south, venturing beyond their territory mainly to trade with foreign peoples for non-local goods. Throughout most of their early history, they remained highly insular, conducting raids only against those who strayed too close to the forest borders. Bastaernian society was divided into hundreds of petty kings, each ruling a tribe or even a subdivision of one. This highly decentralised structure led to frequent conflicts between tribes, and when combined with a strongly warlike culture, resulted in near-constant internal warfare. As a consequence, the Bastaerni were often too occupied with fighting one another to mount sustained campaigns against external enemies. On several occasions, however, the tribes united under a single leader, usually in response to large-scale foreign invasions. These rare unifiers were remembered as harsh and uncompromising rulers, willing to employ brutality to impose unity. Such periods of consolidation never lasted longer than a single generation. Upon the death of the unifying king, tribal autonomy reasserted itself and internal conflict resumed. The only tribes known to have achieved lasting unification were the Osterwaldi and the Suari, despite being ancient enemies and blood rivals. Their mutual hostility was rooted in myth, tracing back to the legendary founders of each tribe, whose conflict was believed to have set the pattern for generations of rivalry. As northern foreigners began establishing colonies along the northern coast, the Bastaerni developed an ambivalent relationship with them. At times, they raided colonial forts and settlements; at others, they engaged in trade. Through this exchange, the Bastaerni entered wider continental networks, exporting furs in return for luxury goods such as silks, dyes, and metals. The Achaeans and Tiberians frequently attempted to manipulate Bastaernian politics by financing and arming rival tribes, deliberately exacerbating internal divisions. Despite these efforts, neither power succeeded in conquering the southern forests. Military expeditions routinely ended in defeat at the hands of skilled Bastaernian raiders, or collapsed due to supply shortages in devastated, food-poor terrain. As a result, the Bastaerni maintained their independence throughout the pre-imperial period.
### Great Shattering (0 - 10) Main Article: Great Shattering The Great Shattering reached the Bastaerni later than most continental societies, largely due to their isolation. When it did arrive, its effects were devastating. The Bastaerni possessed little resistance to the Nasyrik Plague that had long circulated through the Tiberian Empire and surrounding regions. Entire tribes were wiped out as disease spread rapidly through coastal settlements and inland routes. Mass flight followed, as survivors moved southward into the Bastaerni heartlands. This displacement often worsened the crisis, unintentionally spreading infection further into previously unaffected regions. In response, several tribal kings implemented harsh but effective containment measures. These typically involved the execution of the infected and the strict isolation of corpses from the wider population. Through such measures, a small number of rulers survived the Shattering and absorbed neighbouring tribes that had lost their leadership entirely. The cost of survival, however, was immense. The northern forests had been largely destroyed by the divine disasters associated with the Shattering. Climatic changes eventually rendered the southern lands uninhabitable, forcing the surviving Bastaerni back north into territories now largely devoid of human life. From this upheaval emerged two dominant successor kingdoms: the Osterwaldi and the Suari, both settled along the northern coasts and increasingly turned their attention outward, carrying their ancestral rivalry with them.
### First Lude (10 ~ 100)

Pre-Unification Kingdoms (10)

Osterwalde
Main Article: Osterwalde The Osterwaldi traced their origins to the legendary king Walde, who claimed descent from the earth god Aemok. According to tradition, Walde wrestled and slew a mountain lion sent by a jealous goddess whose advances he had rejected, saving his kin and taking the beast’s pelt as a trophy. The lion subsequently became the symbol of the Osterwaldi banner, representing victory over divine opposition and the enduring strength of the clan. Osterwalde emerged as one of the most powerful Bastaernian kingdoms. Its rulers emphasised martial discipline and territorial defence, preserving ancient tribal customs while aggressively contesting borders with rival clans.
Suaring
Main Article: Suaring The Suari descended from the legendary king Suara, who claimed lineage from the god of rivers. Suara was remembered as both the founder of his people and as a challenger of the divine. According to myth, he boasted of being the greatest swimmer, breath-holder, and fisherman in the world, provoking the local river god, who took the form of a turtle. The god challenged Suara to a series of contests, wagering the finest woman in the world against Suara’s life. The trials lasted for weeks, but Suara ultimately prevailed, defeating the river god and securing both his survival and his legendary status. In the post-Shattering period, the Suari kingdom became known for adaptability and cunning. Its rulers exploited river networks for trade and raiding, while maintaining a long-standing blood feud with neighbouring powers.

Auduin's Bastaernian Empire (10 - ?)

Kaiser: Auduin
Unification
Following the Varian–Prythonian War, King Auduin of Osterwalde turned his attention toward the Suari kingdom. His approach mirrored earlier attempts at consolidation within Osterwalde itself during a period known as the Swift Panic. Guided by his advisor, the landowner Æthelstan, Auduin promoted the idea of a shared Bastaernian identity, founding a pan-Bastaernian university and proposing jointly administered cities. Negotiations culminated in a historic meeting between King Auduin and Queen Mara of the Suaring. Rather than conquest, the two rulers agreed to dissolve their respective kingdoms and establish an entirely new political entity. During the unification ceremony, the earth god Aemok was said to have appeared, enraged by the peaceful joining of rival peoples and demanding blood. Æthelstan stepped forward to face the god, declaring that he had lived to see his vision complete. When Aemok reached for his axe, it was found missing. Auduin struck Æthelstan with the divine weapon, severing his right hand rather than killing him, and declared that this sacrifice would be the only blood spilled for Bastaernia. No voice was raised against it. Auduin was proclaimed Kaiser of the Bastaernian Empire, and Æthelstan became his closest advisor, remembered thereafter as the Kaiser’s 'right hand'. Under Auduin’s rule as emperor, elements of foreign administrative practice were combined with traditional tribal values. The empire was divided into semi-autonomous states, each governed by its own leader. This structure secured long-term stability and marked the transition of the Bastaerni from fragmented tribes to a unified empire.
The Elyshi War
Main Article: Elyshi War The Elyshi War did not begin through territorial ambition or long-planned aggression. In Year 26, Loki manifested and killed Regulus — son of Achaean king Ikaros, and the leading figure of the Eldraean faith. What followed was not a war in the calculated sense, but grief wearing armour. Ikaros moved against Bastaernia with a ferocity that caught Auduin off-guard. The two had been allies, and the assault that came reflected a father's rage more than any strategic design. Auduin met it with the measured discipline that had defined his reign, organising his defences and absorbing the Achaean advance where he could, all while understanding well enough what was driving the man on the other side. Bastaernia lost ground steadily. The empire had not been built to withstand a friend. By Year 28, Achaea held the upper hand. A final push might have broken Bastaernian resistance entirely. Ikaros halted instead. The Treaty of Reconciliation returned borders to their pre-war positions and asked nothing of either side beyond that they not fight again. When the two rulers met to sign, those present recalled that they spoke briefly of the years before, and then put their names to the page. Bastaernia withdrew its protection of the Elyshi under the treaty's terms, and ceased to recognise the sect as a legitimate offshoot of Aemocism. What had once sheltered under imperial backing was now left to whatever it could hold together on its own. Notable Bastaerni:

Aemocism

Main Article: Aemocism Bastaernian religion centred on Aemocism, a mythological tradition whose surviving texts recorded the plights of the gods and their interactions with other beings—humanity, the jötnar, and others who served variously as friends, lovers, foes, or kin. The cosmos was understood as seven worlds flanking a central sacred tree, Læraðr, whose roots and branches connected all planes of existence. The pantheon was large, but several gods held particular prominence in Bastaernian worship. Aemok, the earth god, was the most widely venerated. Wielding a battle-axe, he was regarded as the protector of humanity and an unrelenting fighter against the forces that threatened it. His son Loki was celebrated as a cunning seeker of knowledge who traversed all seven worlds and bestowed the runic alphabet upon humanity. A feathered cloak-clad goddess rode to battle to choose among the slain. A vengeful hunting goddess shunned the sea, preferring the wolf-haunted mountains of winter. A god associated with weather and farming was seen as a bringer of peace and plenty. A widowed goddess kept the apples of eternal youth, guarding them against those who would take them by force.

Elyshi

Main Article: Elyshi The Elyshi were a sect of Aemocism that emerged in the southeast of Bastaernia, centred on the worship of Loki above the broader pantheon. Where mainstream Aemocism distributed veneration across its gods, the Elyshi elevated Loki as the primary divine figure, interpreting his pursuit of knowledge and his traversal of the worlds as a mandate for expansion and the construction of sacred spaces. The sect maintained a strict internal hierarchy of five ranks: disciple, priest, high priest, head priest, and apostle. Advancement through the ranks was tied to demonstrated service—recruiting new members, overseeing the construction of altars, temples, and grand temples, and participating in the governance of the faith. The higher ranks held seats on a high council, through which doctrinal and architectural decisions were made collectively. Apostles, the highest rank, were distinguished by personal ceremony and permanent commemoration within the sect's most sacred sites. The Elyshi maintained strict control over their holy sites, permitting entry only to members or those under the direct supervision of senior clergy. Non-members who entered without sanction faced serious consequences under Elyshi law. This territorial strictness reflected the sect's broader outlook: the faith was expansionist in ambition but jealously protective of what it had built. Though it originated independently, the Elyshi faith found its largest following among the Kurgan and within Bastaernia itself. Together these two peoples accounted for the vast majority of its adherents. --- ### Customs

Warrior Ethos

Bastaernian culture placed a high value on physical strength, martial skill, and hard labour. Formal education was limited and largely confined to those of wealth or high birth, while most Bastaerni were born into working families and learned a trade from an early age. Even among the better-off, schooling remained modest by the standards of neighbouring peoples. What the Bastaerni emphasised instead was endurance—in battle, in work, and in the demands of daily life in a society shaped by centuries of internal conflict and environmental hardship. --- ### Nomenclature Bastaernian names drew on a tradition rooted in Old Germanic and Norse linguistic patterns—a reflection of the real-world inspirations behind the culture, though within the world these names belonged to a distinct Bastaernian tongue. Names evoked strength, kinship with the land, martial prowess, and ties to the divine. They were not merely labels but carried meaning: a child named Wulfstan carried the wolf and the stone with them, while one named Auduin was marked from birth as a wealthy friend. Male names tended toward themes of protection, ferocity, and leadership—as with Arnulf (eagle-wolf) or Sigfrid (victory-peace). Female names more often suggested resilience and natural harmony—Ælfwynn (elf-joy) or Ermintrude (universal strength)—though the underlying spirit of endurance ran through both. Most individuals bore a single given name, sometimes accompanied by a clan descriptor or earned epithet reflecting their deeds or lineage. --- ===
