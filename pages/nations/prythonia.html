# Prythonia ## About Prythonia was a highland kingdom of the northern continent of [[Ayka]], and one of the principal expressions of the broader Galatai cultural family, distinguished from its eastern kinsmen by the harsher terrain it occupied and the harder outlook that terrain produced. Its people were not a unified state in any conventional sense for most of their history—a high king had existed since time immemorial, but his authority was largely ceremonial, with real power resting in the hands of local chieftains who ruled their hilltop fortresses as sovereign in all but name. What bound the Prythonians together was not political allegiance but shared culture: a reverence for the natural world mediated by the druids, a warrior tradition built around the longbow and the night raid, and a set of customs that southern civilizations found alternately baffling and barbaric. The [[Great Shattering]] briefly united Prythonia under a high king with genuine authority, but the next [[shatterings|shattering]] undid what crisis had built, and the kingdom never fully recovered its coherence. Following the [[Cruel Shattering]], Prythonia fragmented back into the tribal structures that had always underlain it, and whatever became of those tribes has been lost to history. Prythonic culture left no clear successor state, though its dispersal was not without trace. [[Eldraeanism]], a faith that locates the divine in natural phenomena and events drawn from the living world, carries echoes of the old Galatai nature worship into the present. More distantly, peoples such as [[Pakaroo]] and [[Meridion]] may carry fragments of Prythonic heritage among their varied ancestries—less a living inheritance than the faint genetic memory of a people who passed through the world and left their mark in ways too diffuse to fully trace. === --- ## History ### Tribal Past (... - 0) The misty hills of north-western [[Tiberia]] were dominated by the Galatai clans, the original inhabitants of much of the continent. The Prythonic people were part of this broader Galatai culture, dwelling in the hilly highlands of the north where the land was less fertile than the lush forests inhabited by their eastern kinsmen. Their hilltop fortresses bore witness to countless wars fought to resist outside invaders: the [[varian-empire|Tiberian Empire]]'s legions, the ever-present [[Kurgan]] to the west, and their close kinsmen to the east. The name Prythonia came from ancient [[achaea|Achaean]] explorers, who noted the difference between these people and their eastern cousins. The Prythonic people painted their skin, from this came the name derived from the Achaean words for 'painted people'. #### The High Kingship From time immemorial there had been a High King of Prythonia, crowned with great ceremony on the [[Sacred Hills of Rhea]]. Yet the authority of the high king remained largely ceremonial throughout most of Prythonic history. Each Prythonic chieftain, when asked whose "kingdom" he lived in, would look around at his hall and his fields and his pastures, his flock and his retainers, and answer "Mine, of course." The hilltop fortresses functioned as independent power centres, with local chieftains exercising practical sovereignty over their own territories. The Prythonians were masters of the longbow, the night raid, and the sudden wild charge out of the woods. They idolised valour, but especially liked it when mixed with a bit of mischief—the cattle thief who could whisk an entire herd into the fog; the champion who dined with a rival tribe, and, regaling his hosts with an anecdote of battle, produced from his bag the skull of one of their kinsmen that he had taken as a souvenir. #### Northern Influences Being further north, the Prythonians developed a much harsher outlook on life than their eastern relatives, shaped by the demands of survival in less hospitable terrain. Their proximity to the northern Kurgan resulted in centuries of raids upon their land. Some Kurgan eventually settled within Prythonic territory and assimilated into the culture, bringing with them a collection of new words that enriched the Prythonic language. - - - ### Great Shattering (0 - 10) Main Article: [[Great Shattering]] The Prythonic people were among those who initially benefitted from the breakdown of the natural order following the Great Shattering. Their hated rival, the southern Tiberian Empire, was nearly completely destroyed in the initial catastrophes. For the first time in centuries, the Prythonians found themselves united under a single High King who possessed actual authority rather than merely ceremonial status. Under his personal command, the Prythonians conducted raid after raid into the declining Tiberian territories, bringing back gold, food, and most importantly slaves to work their own poor soil. For a while, the kingdom grew into one of the richest in the world. The impacts of the [[shatterings|shattering]] eventually spread to the Prythonians themselves. The slaves and food that they brought back to their homeland were infected by the vile [[Nasyrik Plague]]. The Prythonic people were devastated by the epidemic. It started with slaves dying, then their crops and livestock no longer tended to. Finally it fell upon the ruling classes, starved of food and forced to flee to escape infection. #### The Migration Under Cedric A young noble by the name of [[Cedric]] led the surviving Prythonians north, fighting his way through the shattered world to secure a safe haven for the remnants of his people. They lived among the mountains and forests, trusting no one for years, until finally the world reached a new age. Cedric led his people down the mountain and was greeted by a transformed world. The continents had shifted, water levels had risen, and most importantly, there were no armies to oppose him. Leading them south towards the fertile lands of the seas, Cedric settled his people within the forests of another Galatai clan lost to history. Only after the land finally settled and the continent became divided did these survivors begin rebuilding what they believed to be their rightful homeland. - - - ### First Lude (10 ~ 100) #### Age of Cedric (~10 - ?) **King:** [[Cedric]] **Notable Prythonians:** === --- ## Geography ### Settlements [[Noviodunum]] [[Seurri]] [[Tamaris]] === --- ## Culture ### Religion #### Nature Worship The Galatai people worshipped the gods of nature, particularly of the forests, rivers, and highlands they inhabited. Unlike the hierarchical pantheons of southern civilisations, Prythonic religion focused on the divine forces present in the natural world around them. - - - #### The Druids Those who had a close connection with the gods were chosen to become druids. These religious leaders used natural materials to create items that brought the human mind closer to the realm of the gods. These items were usually inhaled or ingested, inducing a state that distorted one's view of the world, allowing some to even speak with divine beings. Due to this connection with the gods, the druids held a high place in Galatai society and were often at the left hand of the king to provide divine judgement. Their influence extended beyond religious matters into political and legal spheres, making them essential advisors to rulers and chieftains. --- ### Customs #### Marriage The Prythonic people had marriage customs that seemed barbaric to the more 'civilised' nations which sat on the southern seas. While they might ask for a parent's blessing or create marriage pacts to secure dynasties, the Prythonic men took a more hands-on approach. To take a woman as his own, a man had to steal away the bride-to-be from her house and take her into the woods where a druid would administer the rites. The Prythonic women would only go with a man worthy of being stolen by. Only the best men were chosen this way. This custom was a display of strength, cunning, guile, and most importantly, bravery. There remained the slight threat of being killed while stealing a man's daughter, yet men took the risk despite the dire consequences. - - - #### Battle Preparations Before going into battle, the Prythonic people took the time to bathe, wash, and dye themselves. They had springs throughout the land for washing, and a number of them dotted the woodland border where they frequently fought off southern invaders. To colour themselves, they used a flowering plant that produced a watery-blue dye, which when applied was liable to scar the skin. These scars were a symbol of strength within their society, serving as evidence of the many battles a warrior had fought in and returned victorious. - - - #### Bow Strings and Greetings A common Prythonic greeting was to put two fingers to one's lips, before raising them up towards the other person, showing one's palm to them. This action symbolised friendliness as these fingers were highly important to every man, being their means of stringing their bow and symbol of strength within the society. This greeting had been used by the Galatai people for centuries. Those without these fingers were treated as outcasts of society, figures only fit for fighting with sword and shield. Due to this, a common tactic used by warring chieftains was to take a man's fingers to ensure that he would not fight against them again. - - - #### Rings of Wealth Instead of keeping purses of gold or hiding their wealth, the Prythonic people wore it in the form of rings on their arms. These arm rings were a status symbol for the Prythonians, showing how wealthy one was and, by extension, how many battles one had won. The rings were made up of a vast array of metals, from common bronze to shining gold, and were a demonstration of the feats one had undertaken. Wealthy leaders of the Prythonic warbands had been known to jangle with each step as the multitude of rings on their enormous arms hit against one another. To be recognised as a member of a man's warband, there was a ceremony where the man was gifted one of the arm rings off of the leader's arms, which was engraved with their emblem. This exchange of wealth tied the men together for life and was an unbreakable bond between warriors. Any man that went against it was shunned in Prythonia, regardless of his personal wealth or status. --- ### Nomenclature Prythonic names drew from the deep Galatai heritage of the people, reflecting connection to nature, martial prowess, legendary kingship, and divine favour. Names carried meaning rather than merely identifying their bearer—evoking landscape features, warrior virtues, or mythological significance in keeping with a culture where honour, courage, and closeness to the land and its gods were paramount. Individuals typically bore a single given name, though chieftains and nobles might be further identified by their landholdings or notable deeds. Male names frequently referenced leadership, battle, and the natural world—Ansgar, meaning simply "warrior," and Owyn, "young fighter," spoke directly to the martial life, while Keith, "one who dwells in the forest," and Adair, "from the ford by the oak trees," rooted their bearers in the landscape itself. Names of legendary resonance, such as Arayn and Cedric—"chief"—were common among the nobility. Female names tended to invoke goddesses, natural forces, and sacred qualities—Acionna and Devona echoed the river and spring worship central to Galatai religion, the former a river goddess, the latter goddess of a sacred spring. Names such as Rhiannon, Iseult—"she who must be gazed upon"—and Ygraine carried the weight of legend, while Briana, "strong," and Wynne, "white," reflected the virtues of strength and grace that Prythonic culture held in high regard. --- ===
