Kurgan

History

Tribal Past (... - 0)

Throughout history, the Kurgans have been seen as barbaric and uncultured, that they'd rather take life than give it and that they would start wars with the early winter snow. That is mostly untrue. The people of the north have always had to fight to survive, by nature they have fought to eat and live.

The Kurgan people inhabited the harsh northern lands, where survival required constant struggle against both the elements and neighbouring powers. They battled endlessly with the nations that surrounded them: the Prythonic people to the east, the Tiberians to the south, and the Achaeans to the west. This perpetual conflict shaped Kurgan society into a warlike culture, though one born of necessity rather than inherent savagery.

Great Shattering (0 - 10)

The barbaric tribes of the north were the ones who benefited most from the breakdown of the natural order following the Shattering. Their lands were far from the centre of the disaster, and their warlike societies were well suited to this new world order, allowing them to quickly take advantage of the issues in the south.

After the Shattering, the nations that surrounded the Kurgans rose briefly in wealth as they conducted raids into the broken lands, but eventually became ill with the horrific Nasyrik plague. The Kurgan people initially tried their best to avoid the epidemic, but eventually contracted it themselves. Their hubris got the better of them. Thinking they could profit off others' downfall, they spent more time raiding and gathering riches from the devastated southern territories. What they brought back to their lands instead was the disease itself.

The plague hung to the gold and slaves like hair to skin, and infected many of the men, women, and children. The sickness spread rapidly through Kurgan settlements, claiming countless lives. The Shaman of the time vowed that the gods had forsaken them and that they were left to fend for themselves to redeem their favour with their god.

Migration Under Haraldr

A Jarl of the time, Haraldr of Uthon, defied the word of the King and brought many men and women to join him to new lands. Haraldr knew that the plague would ruin the land and the king with it. He left to spread further north where he would rebuild the Kurgan Kingdom.

He led his followers to icy lands and pine forests. Along the way they found a forest made of pure ice which he knew that many people wouldn't be able to attack, providing perfect natural defence for their new land. This frozen forest became a defining feature of the new Kurgan settlement, offering protection and establishing their presence in the far north.

The Kurgan people established themselves in their new northern territories, maintaining their traditional warrior culture while adapting to the transformed world. The ice forest discovered by Haraldr served as both a natural barrier and a symbol of Kurgan resilience, allowing them to defend their lands while recovering from the devastation of the plague.

Religion

The Kurgans practiced traditional northern polytheism, with worship centred on gods associated with war, survival, and the harsh natural environment they inhabited. The Shaman held significant religious and political authority, serving as intermediaries between the people and the divine. Their pronouncements carried weight in matters of both spiritual and practical governance.

Religious practice emphasised the favour of the gods, which could be earned or lost through the actions of the people. The plague was interpreted by the Shaman as divine punishment for hubris, reflecting the belief that the gods actively judged and responded to human behaviour.

Customs

Battle

There were many individual rituals a warrior might enact before battle, but some of the more widely performed and seen were those acted by the shaman, the Jarl, or King.

The Jarl sliced a mark into the dominant arm of the man or woman at the head of the march. This marked strength and higher rank, but also tested the warrior in battle to see if they could withstand a handicap in combat.

The couple that stood at the front of the raid cut each other, wherever they liked, and then licked the other's cut, making them connected in life and possibly into death. If they were split up in battle, they remained together through this bond.

Before a raid, each family painted each other with runes using the blood of their totem animal. Every family had their own totem animal which they were required to hunt at least twice each year. They then dipped the blade of their weapons in what remained. The family collected the blood over a year and kept it protected, or risked that the gods would give them bad luck in the fight ahead.

Some berserkers lived out in the woods a week before a raid to encapsulate their family totem animal. This put them in a hypnotic, animalistic state that allowed them to go into a frenzy during battle. Sometimes wearing nothing but animal furs and heads, they went to war, using their bare hands and teeth to fight instead of weapons and shields. According to legends, they also felt no pain and kept fighting despite being badly injured.

Marriage

Marriage was an important event in society, marking the coming together of two families. The idea of sexual preferences was unknown to the Kurgan people and they believed the gods created them all equal. Men were allowed to wed men, and women to women. Both families observed a certain set of customs in order to ensure a socially acceptable marriage.

The earliest step was that one was accompanied by members of their family to the other's family to propose. If the proposal was accepted, a date was set and other details were discussed.

On the set date, a huge feast took place which was attended by the families of the two as well as other guests. The feast continued for at least three days and then they pronounced vows of fidelity to each other. It was also a custom for witnesses to accompany the couple to their bed.

The higher one sat in society, the more marriage partners they were able to have. Some Jarls sometimes had upwards of three partners. Within the Kurgan community it showed power and strength to have more than one lover.

Execution

The blood eagle consisted of the victim being placed facedown and restrained. An eagle was carved into his back, and then the ribs were severed from the spine with an axe. The ribs and the skin around them were pulled outward to represent the wings of the eagle. Next, salt was rubbed into the wound, with the victim still alive at this point. Then the lungs were pulled out of the body and stretched across the ribs.

As the victim died, the lungs fluttered in the wind, reminiscent of a bird's wings. This brutal form of execution was reserved for the most serious offences and served as both punishment and ritual.

Nomenclature

Kurgan names draw heavily from Norse and Old Germanic roots, reflecting the northern warrior culture and harsh environment that shaped the people. Names carry specific meanings related to natural forces, animals, divine protection, and martial virtues. The naming tradition emphasises strength, wisdom, and connection to the gods and natural world.

Male names frequently invoke powerful animals, divine figures, and concepts of leadership and warfare. Female names often reference beauty, strength, divine favour, and natural elements, though both genders share connotations of resilience and power. Numerical names are rare, with most names serving as declarations of valued qualities or hoped-for characteristics.
