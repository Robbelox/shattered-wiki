Prythonia

History

Tribal Past (... - 0)

The misty hills of north-western Tiberia were dominated by the Galatai clans, the original inhabitants of much of the continent. The Prythonian people were part of this broader Galatai culture, dwelling in the hilly highlands of the north where the land was less fertile than the lush forests inhabited by their eastern kinsmen. Their hilltop fortresses bore witness to countless wars fought to resist outside invaders: the Empire's legions, the ever-present Kurgan to the west, and their close kinsmen to the east.

The name Prythonia came from ancient Achaean explorers, who noted the difference between these people and their western cousins. The Prythonic people painted their skin different colours, and from this came the name, derived from the Achaean words for 'painted people'.

The High Kingship

From time immemorial there had been a High King of Prythonia, crowned with great ceremony on the sacred hills of Rhea. Yet the authority of the High King remained largely ceremonial throughout most of Prythonian history. Each Prythonic chieftain, when asked whose "kingdom" he lived in, would look around at his hall and his fields and his pastures, his flock and his retainers, and answer "Mine, of course." The hilltop fortresses functioned as independent power centres, with local chieftains exercising practical sovereignty over their own territories.

The Prythonians were masters of the longbow, the night raid, and the sudden wild charge out of the woods. They idolised valour, but especially liked it when mixed with a bit of mischief—the cattle thief who could whisk an entire herd into the fog; the champion who dined with a rival tribe, and, regaling his hosts with an anecdote of battle, produced from his bag the skull of one of their kinsmen that he had taken as a souvenir.

Northern Influences

Being further north, the Prythonians developed a much harsher outlook on life than their eastern relatives, shaped by the demands of survival in less hospitable terrain. Their proximity to the Kurgan people of the snow resulted in centuries of raids upon their land. Some Kurgans eventually settled within Prythonian territory and assimilated into the culture, bringing with them a collection of new Bastaerni words that enriched the Prythonic language.

Great Shattering (0 - 10)

The Prythonic people were among those who initially benefitted from the breakdown of the natural order following the Shattering. Their hated rivals, the Tiberians, were nearly completely destroyed in the initial catastrophes. For the first time in centuries, the Prythonians found themselves united under a single High King who possessed actual authority rather than merely ceremonial status. Under his personal command, the Prythonians conducted raid after raid into the declining Tiberian territories, bringing back gold, food, and most importantly slaves to work their own poor soil. For a while, the kingdom grew into one of the richest in the world.

The Nasyrik Plague

The impacts of the Shattering eventually spread to the Prythonians themselves. The slaves and food that they brought back to their homeland were infected by the vile Nasyrik plague. The Prythonic people were decimated by the epidemic. It started with their slaves dying, and then their crops and livestock. Finally it fell upon the ruling classes, now starved of food and forced to flee to escape infection.

The Migration Under Cedric

A young noble by the name of Cedric led the surviving Prythonians north, fighting his way through the apocalyptic world to secure a safe haven for the remnants of his people. They lived among the mountains and forests, trusting no one for years, until finally the world reached a new age. Cedric led his people down the mountain and was greeted by a transformed world. The continents had shifted, water levels had risen, and most importantly, there were no armies to oppose him.

Leading them south towards the fertile lands of the seas, Cedric settled his people within the forests of another Galatai people, who are now lost to the ages. Only after the land finally settled and the continent became divided did these survivors begin rebuilding what they believed to be their rightful homeland.

Religion

Nature Worship

The Galatai people worshipped the gods of nature, mainly those of the forests, rivers, and highlands that they inhabited. Unlike the centralised pantheons of southern civilisations, Prythonian religion focused on the divine forces present in the natural world around them.

The Druids

Those who had a close connection with the gods were chosen to become Druids. These religious leaders used natural goods to create items that brought the human mind closer to the realm of the gods. These items were usually inhaled or ingested, inducing a state that distorted one's view of the world, allowing some to even speak with divine beings.

Due to this connection with the gods, the Druids held a high place in Galatai society and were often at the left hand of the king to provide divine judgement. Their influence extended beyond religious matters into political and legal spheres, making them essential advisors to rulers and chieftains.

Customs

Marriage

The Prythonic people had marriage customs that seemed barbaric to the more 'civilised' nations which sat on the southern seas. While they might ask for a parent's blessing or create marriage pacts to secure dynasties, the Prythonic men took a more literal approach.

To take a woman as his own, a man had to steal away the bride-to-be from her house and take her into the woods where a druid would administer the rites. The Prythonic women would only be taken by a man that was worthy of stealing her, and through this only the best men were chosen. This custom was a display of strength, cunning, guile, and most importantly, bravery. There remained the slight threat of being killed while stealing a man's daughter, of course. But still, men took the risk despite the dire consequences.

Battle Preparations

Before going into battle, the Prythonic people took the time to bathe, wash, and dye themselves. They had springs throughout the land for washing, and a number of them dotted the woodland border where they frequently sparred with the southern invaders.

To colour themselves, they used a flowering plant that produced a watery-blue dye, which when applied was liable to scar the skin. These scars were a symbol of strength within their society, serving as evidence of the many battles a warrior had fought in and returned victorious.

Bow Strings and Greetings

A common Prythonic greeting was to put two fingers to one's lips, before raising them up towards the other person, showing one's palm to them. This action symbolised friendliness as these fingers were highly important to every man, being their means of stringing their bow and a symbol of strength within the society. This greeting had been used by the Galatai people for centuries as a way of showing that one was friendly to another.

Those without these fingers were treated as outcasts of society, figures only fit for fighting with sword and shield. Due to this, a common tactic used by warring chieftains was to take a man's fingers to ensure that he would not fight against them again.

Rings of Wealth

Instead of purses of gold or hiding their money away, the Prythonic people wore their wealth in the form of rings on their arms. These arm rings were a status symbol for the Prythonians, showing how wealthy one was and, by extension, how many battles one had won. The rings were made up of a vast array of metals, from common bronze to shining gold, and were a demonstration of the feats one had undertaken.

Wealthy leaders of the Prythonic warbands had been known to jangle with each step as the multitude of rings on their enormous arms hit against one another.

To be recognised as a member of a man's warband, there was a ceremony where the man was gifted one of the arm rings off of the leader's arms, which was engraved with their emblem. This exchange of wealth tied the men together for life and was an unbreakable bond between warriors. Any man that went against it was shunned in Prythonia, regardless of his personal wealth or status.

Nomenclature

Prythonian names reflect the Galatai heritage of the people, drawing from Celtic and Brythonic roots that emphasise connection to nature, legendary kingship, martial prowess, and divine favour. Names are not merely identifiers but carry meaning related to landscape features, warrior virtues, or mythological significance. The harsh northern environment and warlike culture are reflected in names that evoke strength, intelligence, and resilience.

Individuals typically bear a single given name, though chieftains and nobles might be identified by their landholdings or deeds. Male names frequently reference leadership, battle, and the natural world, while female names often invoke goddesses, natural forces, beauty, and sacred qualities. Both reflect a society where honour, courage, and connection to the land and divine are paramount.
