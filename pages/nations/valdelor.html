Valdelor

History

Early History and Settlement (450~500)

In Verdan, the survivors' descendants had split up into groups, three of which settled, while one kept on traveling and exploring the world. One of the groups that settled were the Valdelori. They settled on the southern continent.

Their first leader, King Elrond, was said to be a direct descendant of the survivors. This did, however, not stop his people from plotting a revolution against him, for they hated monarchs.

The Fall of Monarchy and Fragmentation (500~1190)

The king’s head rolled, and with him fell the last real monarchy of Valdelor. However, the troubles did not stop there. Over the years, many governments failed and were replaced. This went on until the kingdom finally fell apart into many small states and counties. The population quickly realized this was not ideal, but was unable to do anything about it.

Unified Valdelor (1190-...)

Reunification under Arthur of Benton (1190-1200)

One day, Arthur of Benton appeared. He was a great public speaker and managed to get the majority of Valdelori onto his side. The leaders of the states and counties were eventually forced together, and a pact was written.

The freshly united kingdom of Valdelor was reborn. He had brought the long-desired peace but was unable to get rid of the chaos, so he made a plan. He gave lords and governors land to rule as they saw fit, with the limitations only being his word and his taxes. Most of the people who were made governors had not been in positions of power before, but that did not stop him.

Arthur of Benton, however, stepped back and retired soon after his goal of unifying Valdelor had been accomplished.

A Needed Successor (1200)

Born before the time of Arthur of Benton, there was Alexander. Growing up, he saw how only a few of the Valdelori had really adapted to the constant state of change that the kingdom was in; some tried to, but most had given up. Needless to say, most of them knew a leader to unify them all would be needed to end the chaos.

One of the people to become governor under Arthur's plan was Alexander's father, who was given the lands right next to the capital city to rule.

A new king was to be chosen; the governors and lords had to decide quickly, since without a king the kingdom threatened to fall apart once more. Those who had a say in the decision met in the capital city the night Arthur stepped back and chose Alexander to be the new king. The crowning ceremony was held the next morning in quite a rush, so as not to give anyone a chance to even notice that there was no king. Alexander's father died of old age only a week later, but the people of Valdelor had trust in the governors' decision, much to Alexander's benefit.

Nomenclature

Valdelori names draw heavily from the noble and historical heritage of Western European traditions, particularly Greek, Latin, Celtic, Germanic, and biblical influences, often deriving from roots that emphasize virtues like leadership, wisdom, faith, strength, and nobility. Unlike mere identifiers, names serve as symbols of lineage, divine favor, or personal qualities, reflecting the nation's focus on honor, governance, and cultural legacy. References to ancient gods, nature, light, and heroic deeds invoke a sense of grandeur and continuity with the past. Names are typically elegant and timeless, with phonetic elements that convey authority or grace. There is a gendered pattern: male names frequently evoke power, rulership, light, or divine addition, aligning with roles in leadership or protection; female names more often highlight nobility, light, youth, or divine oath, though both can carry connotations of wisdom and resilience. Neutral names, while not explicitly provided, could blend these themes for flexibility in a society valuing tradition and prestige.
